Chapter Eleven

Countdown

I reached the bottom of the stairwell to see Tatl entertaining herself by flipping through some open picture books. Relief shone through her features when she saw I was unharmed.

"See? You're ok! Although... honestly I admit that I didn't know if the astronomer had been affected by Skull Kid's magic, but there was nothing to worry about after all!" Tatl twinkled. "Soooo... did you find where the Skull Kid is hiding?"

"Oh, well thanks for that!" I sarcastically replied. "And yeah I did. He's controlling the moon from the top of the Clock Tower in town."

Tatl huffed in frustration and kicked the book she had been studying shut, a soft thud echoing throughout the small room. "Of course he is! Why didn't I think of that?"

I looked at the pixie in confusion. "What do you mean? Are you saying that we could have skipped this whole mess of a journey? I crawled through a sewer to get here!"

"Look, I wasn't thinking straight, I'm sorry! I don't like to ruminate about stuff like that, ok!?" Tatl snapped back, but there was a flicker of pain behind her words, like a flame shrinking inward.

"What do you mean? Stuff like what?"

"I just...!" Tatl paused and softened her voice. "I just don't like to think about the past. That junk simply distracts from the present..."

We stood in silence. The flickering torchlight painted long shadows across the walls, and for a moment, it felt like the room was holding its breath with us. I understood her hesitation. The past was a wound I carried too—one that had never quite closed. Horrific struggles, torn friendships, betrayals that still echoed in my chest. A different world. A different me.

"I know what you mean," I said, voice quieter now. "The past is also hard for me to talk about… It hasn't been an easy life for me, even before all of this nonsense."

I knelt beside her, folding my legs underneath me, and looked up into her glowing form, haloed in the soft, golden light from the bookshelves nearby. "But if you ever do  want to talk about it, I'm here. You don’t have to carry all that alone. Not anymore."

Tatl hovered there for a long moment, her wings beating slowly, the quiet hum sounding almost like a heartbeat in the stillness. Then, with hesitant grace, she drifted forward and gently rested herself against my shoulder. Her glow softened, no longer sharp and flickering, but calm—like a firelight remembering how to be warm.

"Same goes for you, dummy," she murmured. Her voice was quieter than I'd ever heard it. "You don’t have to keep pretending you're made of stone. I see you. You know... even when you try to hide it."

Something in her words broke through the last of my armor. I let out a small breath, one I hadn’t realized I was holding, and closed my eyes for a moment. A fragile understanding passed between us—not made of grand declarations, but of quiet, mutual scars that hummed in the spaces we dared not speak aloud.

The world outside was ending. The sky was falling. But in that moment, a thread was woven between two broken souls. Not because I was stronger. But because, for once, I wasn’t entirely alone.

"I guess... you deserve to know about it. In a way, a small part pertains to you as well..." Tatl whispered, still resting lightly on my shoulder. She took a breath that shimmered with the weight of memory, and began:

"Before all the bad stuff happened, Tael, Skull Kid, and I were kind of a family. We stuck together because no one else would take us in. We were outcasts, and orphans." Tatl sighed. "On clear evenings, Tael and I would carry Skull Kid up the Clock Tower and watch the sunset over the ocean. We would all laugh, tell jokes, talk about our desires, and just have fun. When night fell, we gazed at the blanket of stars shining with the moon. I'd recite old legends and ghost stories, and we'd be up all night, too terrified or excited to sleep." Tatl paused, her glow dimming slightly with the ache of remembrance. "Those were during the good times..."

"Sounds to me like you were really attached to Skull Kid... what happened?"

Tatl fell quiet, her wings twitching faintly. When she finally spoke, her voice was tinged with bitterness and grief. "That stupid mask happened. As soon as Skull Kid put it on... he changed."

She fiddled with her tiny hands, the motion a shield against the storm she was reliving.

"Skull Kid, well, he was always kinda mischievous. He loved to play pranks on others, but his jokes rarely ever hurt people. One day, we were playing hide and seek in the forest and we were having a great time! Skull Kid was the seeker, Tael and I hid in the bushes a ways away. We'd been waiting for him for a while, when suddenly Skull Kid started calling out to us, saying that he wanted to show us something. When we reached him, we saw a man with a large pack lying unconscious in the grass. There were masks of all shapes and sizes everywhere, and there was Skull Kid, standing over the man's body... and wearing that awful horned, purple mask."

Tatl turned to face the wall, her small body trembling. Her voice had grown thinner, and something in her shimmered like the last leaf on a tree.

"His pranks quickly became more and more violent, and he forced us to participate. Innocent bystanders suffered. We trashed towns and homes. People started to hate us. But despite all that, Tael and I stayed with him, really hoping that he would see the pain he caused and would stop. But he only grew worse the longer he wore the mask."

Tatl sniffled, and faced me again. Her tiny eyes were still glazed over in memory, and in them I saw something raw and unhealed—the heartbreak of watching someone you love disappear inside themselves.

"It wasn't much longer before the guardian deities of this land banded together, and kicked us out of Termina. That's when he changed for the worst," she said softly, her voice laced with a sorrowful kind of reverence. "Skull Kid was filled with a terrible wrath, screaming about revenge, and how everyone would pay. His dark mask began to give him strange abilities, at the cost of his good spirit. He went through the forest we were sent to, and focused his anger and rage on every living being he came across. In his eyes, they were all his own personal playthings..."

Her words hung between us like a bitter mist. The torchlight shimmered faintly in the corners of her glow, and for once, there was no sarcasm, no mockery. Only grief.

Tatl broke her focus and turned to meet my gaze. Her wings gave the faintest flick, not out of impatience, but to keep herself grounded in the moment.

"And that's when we met you. The Skull Kid saw you and your horse moving through the woods and thought it would be fun to mess with a swordsman. We followed, and... well, you know the rest..."

She floated closer, so close I could feel the warmth of her light on my cheek. "Link... I'm sorry for my part. I truly didn't think it would ever go so far..."

Her voice cracked just slightly. Not from guilt, but from the quiet ache of someone finally laying down a burden they'd carried too long. It was the first time I had ever heard such sincerity come from her. I was speechless, a tightness blooming in my chest, but I nodded.

"It's ok, Tatl. Thank you. Thank you for telling me all of that. It couldn't have been easy."

"But you understand, right?" she pleaded, drifting back an inch. "The Skull Kid isn't really evil. I'm positive that it's the mask that's causing him to be this way! We have to help him!"

I looked away. The room suddenly felt colder.

"I don't know... All I can be certain of is that he's going to destroy the world, and I have to stop him. It might not even be possible to help him. I'm sorry."

Tatl nodded, but there was something hollow in the gesture. Like part of her had braced for those words, and still hoped she wouldn’t hear them. She hovered silently for a moment longer, then turned abruptly and zipped toward the passageway leading back through the sewer.

"Well... either way. We can't do anything while we stand around here. We have to get back to town, and to the top of that tower!"

"Wait, why can't we just go the normal way back into town?" I asked, tilting my head. "There's a door leading right outside, up there!" I pointed up the stairs.

"Well yeah... but... I don't want to be spotted by that crazy old man. He could snap at the first sight of me, and then incinerate us with his horrible fire spitting blowtorch!"

I waved her off with a sigh. "He's not crazy. You guys broke his stuff. I'd be angry too! And besides, the Bomber kids down there are probably awake by now. I really don't feel like explaining myself to them. Now, let's hurry outside and head back to town!"

I turned and started up the stairs, the conversation still clinging to my thoughts like morning mist. Behind me, I heard Tatl grumble under her breath.

"Urghh... fine, Miss Prissy. I'll just hide under your cloak until we're back in town."

She zipped underneath the folds of my green cloak, her deep vulnerability shuttered behind sarcasm once more. But I felt the warmth of her light settle at my shoulder, quieter than usual. And I understood.

Some things are too raw to hold in the open air for long.

I slowed as we neared the top of the stairwell and peeked around the corner to the rising platform. Reton was completely focused on his telescope, muttering quietly to himself as his robes gently rustled in the breeze from the open hatch above. I tiptoed toward the door, the wooden floorboards creaking faintly beneath my soft boots. With careful fingers, I eased the latch open and slipped outside.

I squinted against the brilliant afternoon light, my eyes watering as they adjusted after so long in the subterranean gloom. The Star Chamber crowned the peak of a low hill, encircled by tall trees whose canopies murmured with the wind. Gusts tousled my hair and swirled around my green cloak, which still shielded Tatl, grumbling, beneath its fur-lined interior. The breeze smelled of pine and damp earth, tinged faintly with the scent of something floral just beginning to bloom. Below me, the white limestone walls of Clock Town stood tall and quiet, their rough-hewn faces gleaming under the early summer sun. From here, the town looked peaceful—a gem set against the backdrop of an approaching doom.

The Clock Tower loomed in the distance, its long shadow casting a solemn swath over the town’s rooftops. Its four-sided clock face glinted quietly in the sun, unmoving, as if holding its breath. I could almost hear the echo of a bell that hadn’t yet rung. It was now one o'clock.

After taking a moment to breathe in the eerie calm, I gauged the hill's slope. It wasn't too steep, and the grassy incline beckoned like an open invitation. With a running start, I vaulted forward, cloak streaming behind me. The hill was soft beneath my boots, each step muffled by thick blades of grass as I picked up speed. Tatl let out an indignant squeak as she was jostled about in the folds of my cloak.

"Oof! Could you not  treat me like a sack of beans?!"

I laughed, my hair whipping around me as the wind rushed past. The air tasted clean and wild, and for a brief moment, I felt free.

With a final leap, I landed at the base of the hill among a cluster of maple and birch trees. Shadows clung to their trunks in broad, broken shapes. The rustle of leaves was joined by the chirp of unseen birds, and the distant hum of insects waking from the storm's aftermath. I took a few steadying breaths, then noticed the dirt path curving gently upward—a trail I had entirely missed.

"What were you thinking? I'm stuck in your hot, stinky cloak, having a bad time, and you go jumping off cliffs when we have a perfectly good trail to walk down?" Tatl's muffled voice shot from the folds of fabric, furious and faintly wheezy.

"Oh stop whining, that way was faster." I smirked. "Besides, with all your little jokes about me lately, you had it coming. Why are you still in there anyway?"

"Well... I... but..." Tatl sputtered, clearly struggling to defend her cowardice. "Pshh! Whatever... I don't want that dumb old man to see me, who knows what else he has in his arsenal."

I chuckled and stepped onto the dirt path, brushing my hands along the wild grass lining its edge. The trees thinned out as we walked, the ground beneath my boots shifting between packed soil and slick patches of mud. Overhead, the leafy canopy became dense again, blotting out the sun and casting the grove into a cool twilight hush. Mottled pools of light danced between the leaves, catching on dewdrops and spiderwebs stretched between the branches.

The quagmire made the hike back to town a sluggish plod. My deerskin boots were great for stealth and acrobatics, but terrible for trudging through rough terrain. My feet were sucked into the clinging mud with each step, and the dirt path blended in with the bog. The slop forced me to fight to stay balanced and upright, and each pull flung the thick muck every which way. I must have looked like a street urchin, crawling through untold filth from the sewer and now this thick mud. Maybe I could get cleaned up at the Stock Pot Inn if there was time.

I reached the edge of the shaded grove, winded. Finally, on sundried dirt and fields, I decided to rest. I felt Tatl bat my cloak away, and free herself from the no doubt, foul-smelling garment. The pixie gulped deep breaths of fresh air.

"Do you gasp  have any idea how bad it stinks in there!?" Tatl wheezed dramatically, fanning her face.

"There's nothing I can do about it. That's just what happens when you crawl through a sewer," I muttered, flicking splatters of mud off my leather gloves. The sunlight felt like a blessing, warming the chill from my bones as I kicked clumps of muck from my boots.

"Link! Watch out!"

Something deep green flashed in the corner of my eye, and I twisted aside on instinct. A wet, slapping plop  struck the ground where I had just been. I turned, unslinging my bow in one smooth motion, and aimed at the threat.

What stood before me was a man-sized glob of animate slime. Its translucent emerald form rippled with inner motion, like thick syrup barely holding shape. Two bulbous eyes jiggled at the top of its dripping mass, and inside its gelatinous body, I glimpsed the half-digested remains of some unfortunate forest rabbit. The creature pulsed forward in a twitching lurch, shaped like a grotesque teardrop made of toxic jelly.

I blinked in disbelief. "What is  that thing?!"

Tatl zipped up beside me, her wings buzzing in alarm. "That's a Chuchu! Normally they're small and harmless, but lately? Everything’s going wild. These things have multiplied and grown to absurd sizes. This one’s probably been feeding off magical runoff near town."

The Chuchu quivered and lurched again, its body wobbling like a drum of acid-filled pudding.

"Great," I muttered, drawing my bowstring tight. "So what, do I just shoot it?"

"If you want to survive, yes!" Tatl snapped. "Don’t let it touch you! That slime is super acidic while it's alive. You'll roast faster than a Deku nut in a bonfire. Wait until it's down, then back off—it turns inert when it dies."

I narrowed my eyes. "Perfect. Just the relaxing stroll I needed."

The Chuchu wobbled and leapt towards me once more, making sloppy splat sounds that echoed through the clearing like wet applause. I took aim with the magic bow, drew the string back—still amazed at the radiant shaft of white light that shimmered into existence—and let the bolt fly.

FWAAASPLOOSH!

The creature let out a grotesque gurgle as the bolt pierced through its center, erupting into a spray of fluorescent goop. It collapsed into a glistening puddle of green sludge, twitching once before going still.

"Wow... I love  this thing!" I held up the bow with a grin, turning to Tatl.

She bobbed approvingly. "I'd say the Great Fairy knew exactly what she was doing. I mean, sure, Chuchus and balloons are hardly intimidating, but you’ve got real form. That shot was clean. Like you’ve been doing it for years."

I re-slung the weapon with a sense of practiced comfort. "Yeah, back in Hyrule I had a bow from a temple. It was elegant, powerful... one of my favorite weapons. I used it so much, it became instinct....like breathing or swinging a sword. Before I left, I gave it to my sworn brother Darunia and the Gorons as a parting gift."

"Sure are," Tatl replied. "They live up north in the Snowhead mountains. I haven't been up there in a long time though, they're such a pain to traverse if you aren't made of stone."

My thoughts were interrupted as the rattle of wheels and hooves clattered toward me from the dirt road. A rickety, horse-drawn cart came barreling down the path, its wheels squeaking in protest. The driver pulled hard on the reins with a shout, and the horses whinnied to a stop, kicking up a cloud of dust.

"Hooo, easy now!" the man bellowed, his deep voice like gravel tumbling in a barrel. "Hey! Young lady! Over here!"

A boy, no older than ten, stared blankly at the sky while holding a wooden sword in trembling hands. Another man rocked back and forth, whispering a prayer under his breath. The smell of smoke drifted from the town beyond, sharp and acrid.

Tatl whispered to me, "What are you doing! We don't have time for this dummy."

"Yeah, I... ummm... I have family there," I said quickly, eyes fixed on the cart.

The man didn't seem to believe me, but panic overrode his skepticism. He frantically beckoned for me to climb in. "Maybe so, but you should come with us! It's dangerous to go alone! Especially for a skinny little thing such as yourself. There's monsters and bad people running around EVERYWHERE! The guards abandoned the gates! The mayor's mansion is burning!"

"Yeah, I'm positive! I'm sorry, but I have to get going!" I called back, already breaking into a jog.

Chapter Twelve

End of the Beginning

The fearsome wind screamed through the skies, shredding banners and sending debris flying like shrapnel. The earth convulsed with escalating fury, rupturing stone and shattering roads as the terrible lunar ceiling lowered by the minute. The crumbling city around me was a ghost of its former self—barren, broken, forsaken. All who could flee had already vanished, and those who remained were swallowed by the void. Gravity itself seemed unhinged, torn in a cosmic tug of war between the dying world and the monstrous moon, which now loomed so close its craters were visible in terrifying clarity. Chasms cracked open beneath abandoned homes, devouring entire sections of the town like a beast gnashing its teeth.

I stood with Tatl at the base of the massive Clock Tower, the very ground beneath us trembling like a dying heartbeat. From above, the bellowing tones of midnight thundered across the city, each chime sounding more like a funeral bell than a celebration. Somehow, impossibly, the monolithic clock stood unbroken—its ancient face staring defiantly into the eye of the apocalypse. Fireworks launched from its heights, desperate and bright, as if their flaring brilliance could push back the doom that crept ever closer.

I heard the grinding of great gears behind the cacophony and bolted toward the sound. On the ledge Tatl and I had found earlier, a gaping entrance yawned open where once there was only stone. I sprinted up the ramp, wind clawing at my cloak, and threw myself through the doorway. Inside was a dust-caked elevator shaft, dimly lit and swaying with each quake. A single iron lever jutted from the left wall like a relic. I yanked it hard as a distant building groaned and collapsed in a plume of fire.

The doors creaked shut behind us, sealing out the chaos, and the elevator began to rise with a lurch. A wave of vertigo swept through me, amplified by the swirling sensation tightening in my skull. My breath came in ragged pulses. I felt dizzy, unstable, as if the world outside and the world inside me were both unraveling at once. My limbs felt too long, too light, as if I were being remade again mid-ascent.
