Chapter Seventeen

The Southern Swamp

I awoke with a start as something small and solid pelted my knee. Almost tumbling from the high branch, I caught myself just in time to see two odd, hairy creatures fleeing down the road on all fours. A grumpy Tatl protested my sudden movements and flopped out of the green cloak in a tangled mess.

"What's the big idea?" the fairy whined. "Why are we waking up so early? The sun's only just beginning to rise! And I was having such a great dream too…"

"Calm down, I was woken by some hairy beasts throwing stuff at me…" I shushed, staring after the creatures.

A red sun was peeking just over the hills to the east, indicating a storm like before. Looming grey clouds moved in from the north, and damp wind lightly brushed my hair.

"Great. More rain…" I stood and stretched my arms and legs. "Ok, I guess that's as good a wake-up call as anything. Better get moving before the storm washes us away, Tatl."

I knelt down, grabbing hold of the gnarled branch beneath me, and gracefully swung away from the branch. Rolling mid-air, I landed squarely on the path with an agile twist. I was suddenly very grateful for the new supporting undergarments—the jolt of the landing would’ve been brutal on my chest without them.

"Nice...maybe you belong in the circus, too." Tatl flew down beside me. "I almost guarantee that you couldn't do that in your old body!"

"Hmm, probably not. Maybe this form isn't so useless after all." I cracked a small grin and peered down the path. "Wait... look over there! Those are the things that attacked me!"

The furry white beasts stood still and faced us, almost like they were waiting. Their large eyes glimmered curiously, and flared upturned, wide nostrils on their leathery, grey faces.

"Oh, monkeys? They don't really attack people, they're mostly just annoying." Tatl replied nonchalantly.

"Wha? What's a monkey? We don't have any of those in Hyrule."

"Those animals right there, like I said! Monkeys live in a giant tree within the swamp and are friendly enough, but can be pretty mischievous. I've never heard of them coming out this far though… it's odd for them to wander from their home tree."

"Well, they seem to be waiting for me. If they are friendly, do you think we should follow them?"

"They are heading in the direction of the swamp, so I don't see any reason not to." The pixie shrugged. "But be on your guard, who knows what waits ahead."

I nodded and stepped toward the monkeys, who immediately burst into motion—jumping excitedly, clapping their hands with rapid, rhythmic slaps, then dashing down the dirt road in a flurry of scampering limbs. Their shrill chattering and the soft thump of padded feet against the muddy path echoed through the morning hush, beckoning us to follow with an almost gleeful urgency.

A fine drizzle began to fall from the bruised sky, each droplet ticking softly against leaves and soil as we neared the trail’s end. The sound mingled with the rustle of wind in the branches above, whispering a quiet warning of the storm to come. The monkeys scrambled up some trees that lined the edge of a foul, murky bog, as if marking the threshold between the familiar trail and the swamp’s shadowy heart. A powerful stench permeated the humid air, and a thin mist swirled along the damp, mossy ground. Croaks and growls from swamp creatures resounded all around us, including what sounded like wolfos calls. Large tree trunks supported a wide canopy over the tepid, algae-crusted waters, with vines hanging down various branches. The monkeys took hold of the vines and swung away into the fog.

"Umm...ok? Now what? They can't expect us to follow them like that!" I looked to Tatl.

"I dunno...monkeys are weird. But there's a boat rental shop not far from here, according to that sign. It looks like it's pointing down the shoreline that way." She pointed a tiny arm to a signpost nailed to a tree.

"Good, a boat would be helpful. I'd rather not have to swim through this stuff. The smell alone might kill me!" I reeled away from the squalid bog.

Tatl fluttered down a somewhat obscure pathway through the mire, her glow muted beneath the thick canopy and the ever-churning mists. I chased after her, hair clinging to my cheeks in the warm humidity, my boots sinking into the spongy moss and muck. The air was pungent with rot, the distant croak of frogs and whisper of unseen things all around us.

I caught up just as she froze midair, wings humming with tension. She stared ahead, and I followed her gaze—only to find three grey wolfos hunched over a fallen monkey, their blood-slick muzzles tearing at limp limbs. The sound of crunching bone was too loud.

I motioned quietly to Tatl to back away, my heart thudding in my throat—but it was too late. One of the wolves, its snout matted with gore, snapped its head up. Its green eyes glowed like twin curses. With a savage snarl, it howled—a battle cry that sent the others whipping around, their mouths dripping thick threads of saliva.

I fumbled for my bow and fired a trio of radiant arrows. They struck with sharp flashes—but bounced off, fizzing out harmlessly into mist.

"Damn it!"

The alpha lowered its head and charged.

I shoved the bow aside and reached for the Hero's Mask beneath my cloak. My fingers trembled—just for a moment—as they closed around its smooth, too-familiar shape. It pulsed faintly, like it had been waiting. Like it remembered.

I slammed it onto my face.

The shift was violent.

Muscle coiled and hardened through my limbs. My ribs compressed as a deeper breath filled new lungs. My bones ached, grinding into place as the mask fused with me. Confidence bloomed—hot and overwhelming—as my senses sharpened and something primal took the wheel.

By the time the wolfos reached me, I was already moving.

My shield rose instinctively, steel ringing against claw. I shoved the beast back hard, feeling its weight give beneath my strength. My sword slid free, eager in my hand.

"Come on then," I growled, a crooked smirk tugging at the corners of my mouth. My voice sounded deeper now—rougher, edged with something hungry.

Tatl retreated to the treetops with a worried trill, her glow flickering with unease.

The other two wolves circled, hackles raised, low snarls vibrating the air between us. The alpha lunged again, a blur of grey muscle and fury.

I spun into its charge, steel flashing. My blade met its claws in a spray of sparks, and I twisted, dancing just out of reach as the beast stumbled past. The movement felt effortless—almost playful. I laughed, a low, taunting sound that didn’t quite feel like mine.

"Too slow," I hissed, savoring the moment.

The wolfos staggered, its flank wide open. The scent of blood was thick in the air.

With a single, brutal thrust, I drove my sword into the soft flesh beneath its ribs. The creature howled—a short, sharp cry—before collapsing into the mud, its limbs spasming in death.

I yanked the blade free, flicking blood into the black water.

"One down," I murmured, letting the words hang like a challenge. "Who's next?"

The others lunged at once—claws slicing the air—and I backflipped, landing light on my feet in the mire with a muddy splash. My blood roared. Every heartbeat thudded like war drums in my chest. A plan burned in my mind: bait them into a tree and strike when they were stuck.

I slammed my shield with the hilt of my sword, the clang echoing through the swamp like a bell of war. I grinned—savage, wild—as I backed toward the largest trunk I could find. The wolves took the bait.

Three snarling beasts surged at me, eyes alight with bloodlust. My back struck bark. They thought I was cornered.

Perfect.

I dropped low, rolled, and their claws raked empty air inches above my head. Bark splintered behind me as their talons buried into the trunk.

I didn’t even rise—I spun from the crouch, blade extended, a perfect arc of wrath.

Steel sang. Bodies buckled.

My sword carved through ribs and muscle with a wet, sickening cleave, the howl of one wolf cut short in a bubbling gasp. A geyser of black blood burst from its throat, drenching my tunic and hissing where it hit the magic-etched steel. Gods I loved this sword!

Another wolf twisted, impaled mid-leap, its momentum carrying it down the length of my blade until I shoved it off with a boot to its chest. Bone cracked beneath my heel.

The last one barely had time to flinch. I pivoted, snarling, and split it from jaw to sternum in one downward stroke. The flesh parted like overripe fruit, spraying gore across the roots.

Their lifeless corpses hung grotesquely from the bark, claws still buried deep, as if frozen mid-hunt. The air reeked of iron and rot.

I stood slowly, chest heaving, the thrill still burning hot in my lungs—and laughed. It wasn't relief. It wasn't joy. It was wild and sharp, a sound that didn’t belong in the throat of a child. Loud, long, unrepentant. It echoed through the swamp like a dare—like I was begging the world to send me more.

This... this was what I was made for.

The blood, the precision, the raw satisfaction of dominance. It had been too long.

Too long since I felt alive  like this.

I wiped the ichor from my blade with the sole of my boot, watching the thick smear slide across the steel. Something deep inside me thrilled at the mess, at the power. And for a moment... I didn’t care who I had to become to keep feeling this way.

"That was incredible! How'd you do that?" Tatl praised, flitting down to eye level, her light flickering with hesitant awe.

"Experience," I said, my voice husky with adrenaline. "I’ve fought worse. Wolfos are nothing compared to what I’ve survived."

I stalked past the corpses without looking back, like they were already fading into memory.

"Well, I bet if Skull Kid saw you do that, he'd think twice before messing with you again, haha!"

"He caught me off guard," I muttered. My hands twitched at my sides. "But I wasn’t expecting such power from a simple skull child. Next time, the imp won’t be so lucky."

Tatl hovered awkwardly. "Uhhh, right. We should keep moving though, it's creepy around here…"

"What? Did I say something wrong?" I snapped, quicker than I meant to, the fight still sizzling under my skin.

"No, we just uhh... need to hurry…" she said, shrinking from me, her glow dimming.

"Ok...fine. Be that way," I muttered, forcing my breathing to slow.

Tatl stopped suddenly, wings still.

"It’s just... you get so worked up about killing Skull Kid. He used to be your friend, remember? And... I dunno. Sometimes it sounds like you enjoy  it. The violence. Like you want  it, when you wear your mask…"

Her voice was soft, but it cut deep. And for a second, I couldn’t tell if I was angry because she was wrong—or because she wasn’t.

I stared ahead into the mist, her words echoing longer than they should’ve. Was I really that bloodthirsty now? That wasn’t me. That wasn’t supposed  to be me. I only fought when I had to—only when there was no other choice. But the heat in my blood… it hadn't felt like necessity. It had felt like hunger.

I turned to her, slowly. “Does it really look that way to you?”

“Yeah, it does. It’s kinda scary, to be honest,” Tatl replied, her voice quiet but steady. “And you only act that way when you wear the mask. Without it, you’re calmer. Softer. Like yourself again. I dunno…”

“Huh…” I looked down at the gauntlet on my hand, at how the metal gleamed like it belonged there. “Maybe this form is getting to my head.” I flexed my fingers, half expecting to feel claws instead of skin. “Or maybe… maybe I’m getting too comfortable in someone else’s skin.”

“We can ask the salesman,” she offered. “When this is over. He might know more about it.”

I hesitated. The power still coursed through me. I didn’t want  to let it go. But her eyes held no fear—just concern. Real concern. And that pierced deeper than her words had.

“Yeah… better save the magic anyway,” I mumbled, forcing a half-smile. “No sense burning it all now. As much as I… want to stay like this.”

I paused, glancing down at my hands again. “Tatl… what if I’m changing? Not just the body—me. What if the mask is starting to rewrite who I am?”

She hovered closer. “I don’t think it’s rewriting you. But maybe… it’s amplifying something. Drawing out parts of you that you try to keep buried.”

I swallowed. “But what if those parts take over?”

“They won’t,” she said gently. “Because you’re scared of that. And the fact that you’re scared means you’re still in control.”

With a slow breath, I reached up and peeled the mask away. It came off reluctantly, like it had grown roots in my skin. As it separated, I felt a strange tug in my chest—like something tearing loose from inside. Then the shift began.

It was never graceful.

My shoulders cracked inward, collarbones narrowing. Muscle retreated down my limbs like the tide, leaving me smaller, lighter, softer. My hands trembled as calluses vanished, replaced by thinner fingers that no longer fit the gauntlets quite right. The armor sagged on me—suffocating and oversized, pressing down on shrinking bones with the weight of someone I no longer was. I staggered under it, each step an effort, until the enchantment caught up and the fabric of my clothes adjusted with a faint shimmer. The mail tightened, resizing, reshaping—but not before I felt that moment of utter helplessness, like drowning in the remains of someone else’s body. I stumbled, breath hitching as my center of gravity shifted, hips subtly widening, my chest compressing and rising in uneven spasms as it adjusted to a new rhythm.

My voice, when it finally escaped in a ragged breath, was higher again. Familiar. Unfamiliar.

The worst part was... I was starting to get used to it.

Not just the pain, but the shape. The lightness. The way the world looked from here.

And that terrified me.

Tatl floated close and rested a tiny hand against my shoulder. “We’ll figure it out, okay? You’re just… adjusting. Swapping bodies like that—no one could do it perfectly. Not even you.”

Her glow pressed gently into my shoulder—not heat, not light, but something else. Like the memory of a hand I hadn’t held in years.. It surprised me—how gentle she was being, how much she cared . Tatl had always been sharp-tongued and fast to criticize, but there was something softer in her now, something she rarely let anyone see.

And I felt it. I needed  it. That tiny hand on my shoulder was the only thing keeping the fear from swallowing me whole.

“I don’t know who I’m becoming,” I whispered, feeling my hands tremble as they gripped the wood of my old self.

“Maybe you don’t have to know yet,” she said. “Maybe it’s enough that you’re still trying  to be you.”

I nodded, not trusting my voice, and tucked the mask away inside my cloak. Its weight felt heavier than before.

And we kept walking. Quietly. Just two souls in the mist, trying not to lose sight of who we were…trying not to think about who I was becoming.

Putting any further bleak thoughts aside, I concentrated on our surroundings. The stillness gnawed at me. The swamp had become deathly silent—no croaks, no wingbeats, not even the buzz of insects. The air grew colder, heavy like wet wool against my skin. Each step felt like it stirred ghosts beneath the mire.

Then I saw it. A thick wall of fog rolling in across the lagoon, not drifting but hunting . It pulsed with slow, unnatural rhythm, as if it were breathing.

A chill lanced down my spine.

"Tatl! TATL!" I hissed, trying to keep my voice low but urgent. "Get off the path! Something's coming from the swamp!"

She turned, and the moment her eyes locked onto the approaching haze, her glow dimmed to a nervous flicker. We scrambled off the trail and ducked behind a clutch of enormous, slime-slick ferns. My heart thundered in my ears as we crouched low.

The fog roiled like boiling tar, and then began to congeal —not fade, not part, but thicken , pulling itself together as if it had a mind and malice of its own.

Then we heard it.

A moan—not human, not beast. Long, hollow, and soaked in agony. It pierced the silence like a blade, sending ripples through the swampwater. Every instinct in me screamed to run.

The fog convulsed one final time before vomiting out a shape.

It stumbled forward on two legs—barely. A towering, hunched figure cloaked in rotting swampmoss and tattered black fabric, parts of its flesh blistered and seamed like melted wax. Its face was hidden behind a cracked mask painted in crimson spirals, warped by moisture and time. Veins of shadow throbbed through its limbs, pulsing like worms beneath paper-thin skin.

In one hand, it clutched a diamond-shaped shield crusted with dried blood and mold. In the other—a rusty, jagged sword, its edge curved like a butcher’s hook, pitted with decay. It dragged the blade along the muck as it moved, and the sound it made—metal on stone, on bone—rattled in my teeth.

It didn’t speak. It didn’t need to. Its presence alone suffocated the air, like a memory of death come walking.

Tatl pressed against my shoulder, trembling. "What... what is  that?"

I didn't answer. I couldn't. My mouth had gone dry, my fingers twitching toward my mask.

Because whatever this thing was, I knew in my gut—we weren’t supposed to see it.

And it wasn’t supposed to be awake.

Tatl muffled a sharp cry at the ghastly creature's appearance. The brackish skin dangled in tattered shreds from rotting muscle tissue and bone, and a horrendous stench of death emitted from the body.

The mask worn by the monster looked ancient—less like it had been crafted and more like it had grown  out of time itself. Its wooden surface was splintered with rot and age, warped by moisture, and painted with flaking white tribal patterns that writhed faintly in the shifting fog as though still alive. Three long, moss-streaked green pinions jutted from the crown of its head like the plumage of a diseased bird. The mask's gnarled eyes and mouth were hollow, bottomless voids that seemed to drink  the light around them.

From those gaping orifices dribbled a thick, purple-black ichor, syrupy and reeking of decay. It oozed down the warped mask and spattered onto the bog, hissing softly as it poisoned everything it touched.

The creature’s torso was a massacre of torn flesh. Great wounds split it open from sternum to navel, revealing necrotic organs sloshing wetly with each shambling step. Its intestines dangled like rotten vines, swinging against the remnants of shredded, ceremonial robes that clung to its form in tatters. Each moan that echoed from its throat was guttural—like something being dragged from the bottom of a grave.

Behind it, a slick trail of that noxious ichor bled into the swampwater, curling through it like ink in milk—clouding, poisoning, killing .

The creature staggered deeper into the bog, dragging its sword like a reaper's scythe behind it. Only once it vanished into the veil of mist did the air begin to shift again. The fog clung like breath from a grave, curling around roots and stones with the hunger of something that remembered the living. The stench remained, but the pressure in my chest lifted, and I realized I’d been holding my breath.

Tatl and I exhaled in unison.

"Ughhh... I guess we found our monster," I murmured, stepping slowly out of cover, every muscle still tight with dread. My voice sounded thin, stretched taut across the fear still clinging to my ribs.

"Yeah... let’s umm... not  go after it though," Tatl said, trying to sound flippant but failing. "That thing was terrifying! How could anything live  here with nightmares like that  lurking around!?" She gagged behind me, wings flickering erratically.

"We have to go after it," I said flatly, eyes still scanning the fading mist. "That’s the whole reason we’re here."

"But... did you see  it? That thing was HUGE!  It’s not just some common wolfos or chu-chu—its weapons were, like, twice your size! You’d be paste  before you even touched it! And it was already dead! How do you kill something that’s already  dead?!"

"Trust me, I’ve fought bigger," I replied, more calm than I felt. "And undead aren’t the hardest to put down. They don’t think. They just follow pain. But that fog it seems to travel with... that’s the real problem. If we don’t track it to its lair, it could vanish again."

Tatl buzzed beside me, fidgeting midair. "Seriously? You’re not even the tiniest  bit scared? That thing  will be starring in my nightmares for years!"

I let out a breath, glancing back the way the monster had gone. "I’ve seen worse. Once, I had to descend into the Shadow Temple—an ancient burial vault beneath a graveyard. It wasn’t just ruins... it was a place of ritual torment. The walls were painted in dried blood, iron masks still nailed to the stone where the skeletal remains of prisoners had been bound. There were pits lined with spikes, corridors that whispered.... The dead didn’t just lie still down there—they waited . And don't even get me started about something called the Dead Hand..."

Tatl’s glow pulsed nervously. "W-wait, what?! If that wasn’t  a blatant lie... how are you even still alive ?!"

I shrugged, a bitter smile tugging at the edge of my mouth. "Courage. Luck. Or maybe just a magical sword that cut through evil monster souls like nothing."

She hovered closer. "Let me guess... you conveniently  don’t have it anymore?"

"Returned it to its resting place," I said. "When my mission ended, there was no need to keep it."

"No need ?!" she squeaked. "Did you see  that thing? We could’ve used  a soul-cleaving sword of destiny right about now!"

"Yeah, well... life’s full of bad timing," I muttered, my hand drifting toward the mask hidden beneath my cloak. "We make do with what we have. I left Hyrule with a sword and shield that a temple priest had blessed to burn with divine light whenever it touched something truly evil but...well, it's probably gone forever."

The sword I carried now—when masked, anyway—wasn't quite the same. It had the right weight, and it sang a familiar note when I swung it, but there was something missing in the resonance. It felt hollow, like an echo of what it pretended to be. Not a legacy blade, not a weapon of destiny—just a shadow made steel. It moved like it remembered greatness but couldn’t quite reclaim it. As if it knew it was only wearing a hero… not living with one.

The thought made my lips curl downward in anger at my fate. The memory of what I once carried—what I once was —sank like a stone in my chest. The world I left behind had crowned me a hero, but here I was, crawling through a haunted swamp with a false sword and a body that barely felt like mine. Every step forward felt like penance. Every breath was like a reminder that no prophecy had saved me from this. My destiny had turned to ash, and I was choking on the embers.

Tatl was quiet for a beat, then mumbled, "I still think you’re insane. But I guess that’s the only kind of person who’d walk into a swamp like this."

"That makes two of us," I said, finally cracking a small smile.

She huffed. "Don’t remind me."

I started down the trail again, anxious to leave the area.

"Wait, you said you completed a mission. What mission?" Tatl pressed, her wings flicking with renewed energy. "You can't just leave it there, you've piqued  my interest!"

"It was a long time ago… I don't really want to talk about it," I muttered, already regretting letting anything slip.

"Oh come on ! You said that before, but I told you a little about my past. Why don't you return the favor?" she begged, drifting close with an expectant tilt.

"Ugh… fine. I guess that's fair." I sighed, rubbing the bridge of my nose. "Okay, here's the short version. A thief named Ganondorf stole a sacred relic that granted his wish to rule the world. He declared himself King of Evil, enslaved the races of Hyrule, and I was chosen to stop him. So I did—with help from... people I trusted."

"That's all …? That's the best you can do? You can't just drop  something like that and not deliver. Gimme more!" she pushed.

I groaned. "Alright. I cleansed evil from seven temples—each one crawling with monsters, traps, curses. Fought through endless hordes, rescued the sages who’d been imprisoned, sealed Ganondorf in a void of darkness... and then got shoved back in time to relive my childhood. Like it all never happened."

"Well... it’s better than nothing," Tatl said, though her voice had softened. "One day I’ll break through that guarded little hero heart, but for now I guess I’ll let you be mysterious."

"Yup. Legendary hero. Or... I was." My voice came out quieter than I meant. "Before I was sent back. I had night terrors for months after. Waking up thinking the world had ended. Zelda… she was there for me. Helped me adjust. Helped me... let go."

"Who's Zelda? She sounds nice."

"She was the Princess of Hyrule. Now the Queen, probably." I tried to sound casual, but even her name stirred something old and aching in my chest.

"Wow. I gotta say, I wouldn’t have guessed you were the ‘falling-for-royalty’ type," Tatl whistled. "All noble and tragic."

"I never said I was in love," I snapped, a little too quickly.

"It was totally in your voice when you said her name, you cannot  deny it," she teased. "And it sounds like she cared about you too. No wonder you're all moody and evasive—you feel guilty for leaving her behind."

"No! That's not...it…" I sighed, Tatl was right. "Look...I just don't belong with a princess. I'm a battle-hardened warrior, and Hyrule is in a time of peace. I can't live like that, not after everything I'd been put through. How could Zelda possibly be happy with someone like me?"

"I dunno, love isn't my strong suit. But I doubt that Zelda cares. I think when all this is over, you should go back and tell her how you feel."

"I don't even know how to get back, let alone survive this place! Besides, look  at me!"

I gestured to my feminine form and kicked a rock in frustration. Pain rippled through my foot and I stumbled, hopping on one leg as I massaged my stubbed toe.

"Owww! Arrgh! I'm done talking about this!" I screamed.

My shrill voice echoed through the swamp, startling birds from the branches above in a flurry of wings. The sting faded, but the embarrassment lingered. Not from the pain—real as it was—but from the rawness of being seen while my emotions were falling apart.

"Alright, alright! Sheesh ! Calm down before you summon that bloodcurdling monster again!" Tatl waved frantically, scanning the shadows. "I'm sorry... forget I mentioned anything."

I glared at the ground, blinking away the rain that had fallen into my eyes.

Only... it wasn’t raining.

Tatl hovered closer, her glow dimming into something gentler—but she didn’t speak. Not right away.

Instead, she lingered beside me in silence. And for once, it wasn’t filled with quips or questions. Just quiet understanding. I could feel her watching me—not with pity, but with a dawning clarity. Like she was seeing past the mask for the first time, seeing the shape of someone scarred beneath all the silence.

I didn’t want her to see it.

I did  want her to see it.

I looked down at my hands—smaller now, softer, uncertain—and sighed heavily.

"Maybe," I muttered, eyes fixed on the shifting mist ahead. "Maybe I was in love. Maybe... it’s complicated."

The words felt too small for the weight they carried. Like naming a wound didn’t make it bleed any less.

"Love didn’t stop her from turning back the clock. It didn’t hold back… fate. And it sure didn’t stop me from getting torn out of one life and thrown into another." I paused. "She…ugh…she did what she thought was right. Maybe she was  right. I just... I don’t know where I’m supposed to put any of it now."

Tatl’s voice came at last, quiet and low. “Maybe… you don’t have to. Not yet. Maybe it’s okay if it doesn’t make sense for a while.”

I glanced at her.

She didn’t look away. “You’ve been carrying all that without a break, haven’t you? Always trying to be the hero, even when no one’s looking. Even when it’s tearing you apart.”

I said nothing. I didn’t have to.

“You know… um… you’re not alone now,” she said, more gently than I’d ever heard her. “You don’t have to carry it all by yourself. Not with me here.”

Her glow warmed slightly, a soft pulse of light in the gray. Not bright, not bold—just enough to be felt, like a hand quietly reaching out in the dark.

And I found myself—just for a moment—believing her. Wanting to believe her. That someone could stand beside me and see  the ruin without flinching.

"Let's... just keep moving," I said, my voice rough with emotion. Not dismissive—just worn. Like the words were walking on blistered feet.

She didn’t argue. Just stayed close, her glow surreal against the fog. Her silence said more than comfort ever could. She was with  me. No expectations. No judgment. Just presence.

And we kept walking, the mist curling around our legs like memories too stubborn to fade—but now, for the first time in a long time, not entirely alone in them.

I chose to remain quiet until we reached the boathouse. Thankfully, Tatl followed my lead—but her words kept echoing through my mind like ripples over a wound that refused to close. She had seen more than I meant to show. And worse—she’d been right. But admitting that to her? To myself? That was still too much.

I knew how Zelda had looked at me. I knew what we shared, unspoken as it often was. But my happiness... that had been forfeit long ago. Offered up to the gods the moment I drew the Master Sword and stepped into the timestream. Zelda belonged to Hyrule, to peace, to golden rooms and measured words. She deserved the warmth of sunlit courtyards, the laughter of children, soft hands and safer futures. I was none of that. I was the shadow that lingered after the fire. A sword with no sheath. A name whispered in temples and forgotten at home.

I was built for battle and adventure.

Despite the curses that clung to every branch, and the unease that coiled like fog at my ankles, part of me—some twisted part—almost enjoyed  the trek through the bog. There was a rawness here. A thrill beneath the dread. Like walking a knife’s edge with bare feet. The uncertainty hummed through my bones like the prelude to battle. It gave me purpose . Purpose I hadn’t felt since the last seal closed the Demon King in the abyss and silence fell over the kingdom.

Peace had starved me. Six years of it had eroded me in ways no war ever could. Without a threat, I was just... leftover. Rusting.

So I came here. To a place that still bled. Where monsters still hunted and evil still whispered in the dark. Here, I wasn’t obsolete. Here, I mattered .

The trail ended at a long, weathered boardwalk. Planks groaned beneath my boots as I stepped onto it, damp with moss and slick with age. Mist curled like breath around my ankles, coiling up from the dark water below. The air smelled brackish—part algae, part rot, tinged faintly with woodsmoke and something faintly sickly sweet, like an old corpse.

The world fell quiet, as if holding its breath. Lanterns glowed faintly at the head of the pier, casting amber halos into the fog like tired eyes that refused to close. Further down the dock, nestled into the mist, stood an owl statue—weathered, half-covered in lichen, but unmistakable. Its eyes were shut, wings folded, like it was waiting for something.

Across the water, a two-story shack floated gently, tethered by thick ropes to half-submerged pylons. The building creaked softly with each ripple of the swamp, like it was murmuring in its sleep. Smoke curled from its chimney in a lazy spiral, tracing a slow path upward into the gray.

"Alright, here we are. Are you OK, Link?" Tatl chimed, her voice careful—not too light, not too prying.

"Yeah, I'm fine… I just had to work through some things," I said, forcing a breath past the tightness in my chest. "I just want to say… that you were right. I'm not good at talking about this stuff. And I'm sorry. For being such an idiot."

Tatl hovered a little closer, her glow warmer than the lanterns. "Hey... thanks for saying that. You’re not an idiot. Just... damaged goods like the rest of us."

I snorted softly. "Thanks.... I think."

"You're welcome," she said, and for once, we both laughed. Just a little.

And for a heartbeat, the fog didn’t feel so heavy.

The boardwalk swayed gently beneath our steps, the wood groaning with age and damp. Ripples fanned out through the stagnant water with each footfall, disturbing floating debris and clumps of green algae. Gnats and flies buzzed persistently around our heads, drawn to the sweat and lantern glow. I swatted at them half-heartedly, my mind still half-lost in memory. The smell of mildew, river mud, and faint smoke drifted in the air, thick as the mist.

We reached the weather-beaten door of the boathouse, its surface warped with years of exposure. I knocked with my knuckles—three sharp raps—and heard the shuffle of movement inside. A small peephole snapped open, and a gruff voice echoed from within.

"Eh? What do ye want!"

"We would like to rent a boat. Do you have any available?" Tatl answered politely.

"A boat? Nah, this is the photography club. The boat rental’s up the ladder to the left! But ahhh... the owner, Koume, hasn’t been by in a few days!"

The man’s voice boomed with a thick, unplaceable accent, every syllable dragged out like rope soaked in molasses.

"Oh, okay then... do you know any other boat shops around here?" I asked, leaning closer to the opening. "We're kind of in a hurry."

"Nope, 'fraid this is the only place 'round here with boats! Tell ye what though, you two look straight bushed! Why don’t ya both come inside for a nice cuppa 'n warm yer spirits. I’m sure ye can spare a tic after hoofin’ it over 'ere!"

The heavy door creaked inward on rusted hinges, revealing a formidable man with a closely-shaved black beard and mirror-tinted glasses that flashed in the lantern light. A brown leather flight cap was pulled tight over his head, and his massive arm gestured us in with the ease of someone used to taking up space. His vest, worn and sun-faded, clung to a thick, hairy torso and a bulging gut, and beneath it, a pair of comically small white-and-red pinstriped trousers barely clung to his frame.

I cast Tatl a sideways glance and shrugged.

She gave a small nod, and we stepped into the warm, flickering light of the cabin.

"Awright, make yerselves at home. I got the tea goin' on the mantle," the mountainous man bellowed, his voice booming with that unmistakable swamptung lilt.

The cabin chamber was cozy and worn-in, smelling faintly of cedar smoke and damp wool. Dim lanterns hung from exposed beams, casting soft golden pools of light over the mismatched furniture. The walls were cluttered with curling photographs—sepia-toned snapshots of travelers, misty lagoons, and half-submerged ruins. A few taxidermied fish, preserved mid-thrash, hung above a display of homemade bows and rusted tools.

A large couch sagged against the far wall, draped in an old knitted throw. Several leafy potted plants clustered beside a squat little table, their fronds reaching like curious hands toward the heat of the fire. The man waved us toward it, and we obliged, sinking into the creaking cushions.

He thumped over to the fireplace, where a small kettle hissed quietly over glowing coals. He stirred them with a blackened iron poker, sending a puff of embers into the air.

"So, what brings you two birds to this armpit of Termina? We don't get too many fair folk such as yerselves down 'ere," he asked with a broad grin, revealing a missing tooth.

Before we could answer, the man clapped a massive hand over his face.

" Strewth!  How could I be so crass to not make proper acquaintances! I'm called Frank, if it so pleases ye both." He executed a flourishing bow with surprising grace for someone of his size. "How might I call you?"

"Well, my name is Link… and this is Tatl."

"Well, 'tis a pleasure to meet ya!" Frank said, ambling behind a cluttered counter at the other end of the room. He rummaged briefly through a drawer full of mismatched mugs. "So, a boat… are ye here to see th' sights? I'm a tour guide of sorts, and I know me way 'round the swamp like the back o' me clamp!"

"Ummm, kind of, I think?" I replied, still working to decipher his colorful dialect. "We're hunting the monster that's causing havoc here."

Frank paused, mug halfway to the counter. His jovial expression stiffened. Slowly, he leaned forward, elbows bracing against the bar’s edge.

The smile never quite left his face—but it no longer reached his eyes.

"Blimey! Yer serious!" The man scratched his exposed, hairy chest with thick fingers. "You best stay away from that beast, sheila! Matter o’ fact, ye best flee this bloody swamp altogether!"

"It's Link," I said sharply, straightening. "And we can't leave. I can handle myself, and we were sent to deal with the monster." Tatl hovered beside me, nodding in solidarity.

"I apologize for me swamptung... 'Sheila' is just a word we use down 'ere for spunky young lasses such as yerself," he said with a sheepish grin. "But I’m bloody serious—ye gotta get out while ya still can. Odalwa ain’t an ordinary monster."

"Odalwa?" Tatl echoed, brow furrowing. "That thing has a name ?"

"Yeh. Used to be just a ghost story we told the wee ones to keep 'em from wanderin'. ‘Bog in to yer rat stew, Bill and Sally, or Ol’ Odalwa’ll tear yer tongue out!’ That sort o’ thing. But they ain’t just tales no more. Somethin’ woke it up. It’s real now, and it's been snatchin' folk."

"So… what you're saying is a storybook monster came to life. And now it's hunting people," I said, flatly.

"Right-o! And from what I seen and heard, it favors little sheilas like you.  So I’m beggin’ ya—get outta dodge before it’s too late."

I rose from the couch, jaw tight. "No. Look, if you can’t or won’t help us, we’re going either way."

Frank raised his hands defensively. "Wait jist a tic, mate! Hear me out, yeh? If yer gonna be trouncin’ about the swamp, you’ll need a boat, at the very least. That fancy bow o’ yours won’t do much against Odalwa, if you’re serious about huntin’ it."

"Then what do you suggest?" I muttered.

"Koume’s sister, Kotake, runs a magic shop east of here. Strange old bird, but wise as the tide. She might know where Koume’s gotten off to. And chances are, she’s got some thingummy or charm that might help ye survive out there."

He scratched behind his ear, his tone softening. "I'd be heaps grateful if ya found Koume. Business has been dryer than a dead Deku without my boat captain."

I exchanged a glance with Tatl, who nodded.

"Sounds like we’d be killing two birds with one stone. Where’s the shop, exactly?"

"Coupla clicks east. Stick to the shoreline and you’ll see the path clear as day. Big crooked trees and some glowy fungus’ll light yer way."

"Right. Thank you for your... kind... hospitality, Frank," I said, heading toward the exit. "But I think we’ll have to take a rain check on that tea."

Frank gave a knowing chuckle and tipped an imaginary hat. "Fair winds, then. And may yer feet stay dry and yer bow stay true."

I waved once, and stepped back into the fog with Tatl at my side, the door creaking shut behind us.

"Swamp dwellers… so weird." Tatl shook her head, her tiny voice tinged with amusement and mild exasperation. "I can barely ever understand them—and they're all pretty crazy. But... I guess you'd have to be, living in a place like this."

I laughed softly, the sound a little hoarse in the heavy air, and started down the boardwalk toward solid ground.

"Alright. We should hurry. I can't imagine we have much time left before the moon falls."

Tatl floated upward, scanning the thick clouds overhead for a glimpse of the doomed sky.

"I can't tell either, but you're probably right. He said the shop was east of here, shouldn’t be too far a walk. I’m more worried about what he said about Odalwa…" She glanced around, her glow dimming slightly.

"If we see any fog drifting our way, we’ll hide—same as before. Don’t worry. We’ve made it this far." I tried to sound calm, steady. My boots squished into the mossy earth, the wet ground sucking at each step.

"Okay, you’re the hero," Tatl said, not quite reassured, but choosing to trust me anyway.

She drifted in closer, her light brushing against my shoulder—not a nudge, not an accident. A quiet signal. A promise.

Whatever came next, we’d face it together.

I smiled faintly and gave her a thumbs-up, the gesture small but sincere. She chuckled, a fragile sound that stirred the air around us like wind through reeds.

The shallow mud squelched beneath our feet as we moved eastward into the pale haze of the swamp. Ferns and vine-choked trunks loomed in the fog like the silhouettes of forgotten sentinels. Crickets whispered. Distant birds shrieked once and fell silent.

With each step, the quiet thickened. The squelch of my boots faded beneath the hush, swallowed by the stillness.

And I couldn’t shake the feeling—that every step forward was drawing us nearer to something nameless. Not just a beast or a shadow or a trap, but something older. Something watching.

The mist curled at our heels like ghosts, and even with Tatl’s gentle glow beside me, the weight of the sky pressed down harder with each breath.

Whatever lay ahead—monster, memory, or madness—it wasn’t just a threat to overcome.

It was a reckoning.

And I had to face it—not just for Termina, but for every fractured piece of myself scattered through this dying world. My name, my purpose, my fate… they all waited in that dark. So I stepped forward—into the dread, into the waiting dark—toward the reckoning that bore my name.
