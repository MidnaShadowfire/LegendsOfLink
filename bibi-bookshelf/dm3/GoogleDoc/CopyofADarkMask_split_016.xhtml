Chapter Eighteen

Ambush

The swamplands grew darker and more oppressive as the day died slowly, suffocated beneath a sky of muddy orange and bruised violet clouds. The sunset was smothered completely, hidden by thick stormbanks that loomed in the distance. The deluge promised that morning had never come, but its absence felt like a curse rather than a mercy. Distant thunder rumbled low across the horizon, a constant growl that never ceased, as if some vast beast circled just beyond the horizon, patiently stalking us, waiting for nightfall. Still, the air hung heavy and soured—dense with the stench of algae, molded rot, and something deeper that filled my lungs with dread. Breathing it in made my throat itch. It felt like the swamp was trying to get inside me.

Tatl and I picked our way along the marshy shoreline, boots sucking into damp earth that squelched and gurgled with every step. The buzzing of insects that had plagued us earlier began to fade, replaced by a sinister hush. Birds, too, had gone silent. It wasn’t peaceful. It was the kind of silence that animals used to warn each other that a predator was near. I tried not to dwell on it.

Frank, the strange but hospitable tour guide of the boathouse, had told us of a Magic Shop somewhere further down the bank. According to him, the shop owner’s sister was likely still there and might have the key to the locked boat. We followed that hope. Tatl drifted close to my shoulder, veering away from the more toxic patches of marsh, her gaze darting restlessly into the mist-choked trees as if expecting them to move.

"What if we run into Odalwa on the way to the magic shop? It was bad enough to look at in broad daylight, and I certainly don't want to be grabbed by the monster!" Tatl flitted about nervously, startling at every movement within the mists.

"Don't worry about it, we'll just hide like last time. If it can't see us, then we'll be just fine." I offered a weak grin. "Worst case, I throw you at it and run."

"Excuse me?! I am not bait! I am a dazzling orb of wisdom and guidance! You throw me at a monster and I will personally haunt you in every temple from here to Termina Field."

"See? You’d make a great distraction. Loud and  glowy."

I joked lightly, but a brief pang of guilt twisted in my chest at her glare. Truthfully, I’d sooner fling myself into danger than risk her.

Tatl huffed and glared, but the glow around her pulsed a little warmer, less frantic.

"Frank said the shop isn’t far," I added more gently. "We’ll be there soon."

"He also said people like you were Odalwa's favorite victims. You know—small, brave, tragically stab-happy types. And it’s getting dark… what if it sneaks up on us and kidnaps you like the others?"

"I highly doubt that a big monster could sneak around so easily. Plus, I’ve noticed my hearing’s a lot better since... well, you know." I tapped my temple. "New ears and all..."

"Oh perfect. So we’ll hear  it coming before it eats you. Great comfort."

I tried to keep my tone light for her sake, but truthfully, her fear was starting to creep into my own bones. The longer we walked, the more the shadows seemed to shift and breathe around us. My gut was twisting with unease, a wordless signal screaming from the same primal place that made the animals flee before a storm.

"Hrrmm ok...if you say so. You're the warrior here, not me. I suppose you'd know the situation best." Tatl took a deep breath and blew it out slowly, as if trying to physically push her nerves down. "So... I wonder what happened to the boat lady. Do you think she got taken, like the others?"

"I hope not," I muttered. "We don't even know where they're being kept. Plus we really need a boat here. I wouldn't dare  swim in that stuff!"

Even thinking about it made my skin crawl—murky water thick with algae, debris, and who-knew-what corpses bloating under the surface.

"If she was kidnapped, then there's apparently no way we can get a boat, and we'd be stuck. So please, don't jinx it for us."

"I'm just saying... we should have a backup plan or something…" Tatl grumbled.

"Ugh... fine. I guess you're right." I sighed. "Keep an eye out for anything we can possibly use as a raft. There's gotta be driftwood along the banks we can lash together. But stay away from the treeline—if I were a monster, that's where I'd be lurking."

We scanned the banks as we walked, our eyes flicking between patches of reeds and half-sunken logs, the last slivers of daylight fading fast. The air was still thick and warm, reeking of mold and death. When we rounded a bend in the lagoon, a sickly-sweet stench slammed into me like a blunt weapon. I gagged and glanced around frantically, something was wrong here.

The water ahead had turned a curdled shade of purplish-brown, its oily surface slicked with bloated fish and bits of unidentifiable gore. The banks were mottled with black silt and flies, buzzing in lazy, grotesque swirls.

"Uh, Link? I have a bad feeling about this area," Tatl whispered, barely audible against the dense silence. "Look at the water. Do you think... that thing could be nearby?"

"It's a good chance," I said, scanning the treeline. My eyes caught a fallen log near the shore. "Quick, let’s take cover over there."

We moved fast but low, instinctively hunched, every squelch of our boots in the muck sounding like a gunshot in the hush. I ducked behind the log while Tatl slipped inside my cloak like a trembling ember.

For long moments we held our breath. Nothing moved.

I slowly lifted my head to peek. The lagoon had gone still, deathly still, as if even the wind had stopped breathing. Purple-grey clouds loomed overhead, reflected in the mirrored slime of the water. Algae clumped like floating bruises on the surface. Across the way, a thick copse of moss-draped trees hung in silence, their vines swaying just barely—as if something had brushed past them.

I peered into the trees across the stagnant water, straining my eyes for movement among the gnarled trunks and weeping vines. The air was still, sticky, and so thick it felt like it could be sliced. A sudden explosion of motion snapped my gaze upward—birds erupted from the canopy in a flurry of panicked wings, screeching as they fled from something unseen. The sound faded quickly, swallowed by the thick fog.

The swamp held its breath. Everything stilled to unnatural silence. Then came the footfalls—heavy, distant, rhythmic… Each one sent subtle tremors through the earth, vibrating up through my boots and into my bones. The lagoon's mirrored surface rippled with each impact, breaking apart reflections like shattered glass. My stomach knotted.

I didn’t breathe. Didn’t blink. Just waited.

With a splintering crash, Odalwa burst through the trees—its enormous form exploding into view like a nightmare made real. Its sword, jagged and grotesque, cleaved through low-hanging branches as if they were paper. Its body was painted in crude war markings, masked by a twisted wooden faceplate riddled with nails and carvings. It stomped to the water’s edge, its movements oddly stiff, like a puppet being jerked forward by invisible strings.

Then it sniffed.

Sharp, wet gasps sucked in through the slits of the mask, each one sounding like the gurgle of drowning lungs. It tilted its head left, then right, twitching with each breath. My blood turned to ice as its hollow eye-holes swiveled in our direction.

I ducked behind the log, heart pounding in my ears like a war drum. The wood was trembling against my spine with each of the thing’s steps. Tatl let out a tiny whimper inside the cloak, her glow dimming almost to nothing. I slipped a hand back to my belt, fingers closing around the mask. If it came closer—

A shriek split the stillness.

From across the lagoon, dozens of furious howls echoed, followed by a clatter of thrown stones. The monkeys. A chaotic barrage of screeching erupted from the canopy as rocks pelted the monster’s face and shoulders. I could hear them thunking against its wooden mask—sharp cracks and splinters flying.

Odalwa let out a guttural roar and spun on its heel, the sheer vibration of its voice rippling the water. It charged across the swamp in a furious, thudding sprint, crashing after the monkeys as they scattered into the trees like startled leaves.

I dared to raise my head. The log creaked as I shifted.

Dozens of monkeys—mud-caked, shaggy, and wildly different in shape and size—were perched in the treetops, flinging anything they could get their hands on: rocks, fruit, even bones. The scene was primal and chaotic, but to my amazement, they were holding their ground. The liquid rot from its body spewed into the waters as the monster moved, and the monkeys retreated back into the safety of the woods.

My hand trembled around the mask. That had been too close. One more second... one single misplaced breath...

Tatl stirred faintly in my cloak. "That was way  too close."

"Yeah," I whispered. "I noticed."

When everything was quiet and calm once more, we both breathed a huge sigh of relief.

"See? Whew  Not a problem," I said with a shaky laugh. "We could see it from a mile away! Totally under control."

Tatl zipped out from under my cloak with a scowl. "You were about ten seconds from getting pancaked! If it hadn’t been for those monkeys, we’d be monster chow."

"Well… maybe," I admitted, rubbing the back of my neck. "I could’ve transformed and taken a swing at it. Might be smart to put the mask on ahead of time next time."

"Oh, perfect. Loud thumping and dramatic transformation sparkles in the middle of a stealth mission—great plan." Tatl zipped between me and the mask, glowing brighter in frustration. "Seriously, you’re not exactly Mister Subtle with that thing on."

"Okay, okay! Point taken. But if we bump into Odalwa again, don’t expect a well-timed costume change."

She sighed, drifting back beside me. "Let’s just not  bump into him again. Deal?"

"Deal."

We trudged back toward the shore, following the murky lagoon, the last echoes of monkey screeches fading behind us like distant war cries. Whether they meant to save us or not, I owed them a debt.

Twilight settled over the swamp like a burial shroud. The light drained from the sky, replaced by a hazy gray-blue gloom that seeped into everything—water, bark, skin. The whole world felt damp and grave-cold. Trickling streams bled into the main body of water—sickly green and black, oozing like veins across the mire, carrying with them strange slicks of iridescent oil that shimmered with unnatural colors.

We passed bloated animal corpses half-submerged in the muck, their skin blistered and peeling, limbs stiff in death. Some had sunken eyes filled with maggots, others had mouths frozen open in silent screams. Flies buzzed in maddened spirals, thick enough to form clouds that dispersed only when we neared. Gnats bit at my face and neck, leaving itchy welts that throbbed with each heartbeat.

Bones, long picked clean, jutted from the silt like broken fingers reaching for mercy—or perhaps warning us back. A stench of rot and chemical decay clung to the air, thicker than fog, crawling into my nose and mouth until I nearly gagged. Each breath felt like inhaling poison.

The ground squelched beneath our feet, soft and treacherous. My boot slipped once on a patch of black slime and I caught myself on a twisted root that oozed decayed matter the color of dried blood.

I pulled my cloak tighter, but it didn’t help. Nothing could. The swamp was death—slow, sticky, and inescapable. And it was watching us.

"Ughh.. I'm thinking we should just walk through the trees and forget following the shore! These flies are driving me nuts !" I hissed to Tatl, flailing at the relentless swarm buzzing around my face. They were in my ears, up my nose—one even tried to crawl into  my mouth.

"What about the shop? Frank said to follow the shore—" Tatl ducked as I swatted at a particularly aggressive gnat. "Hey! Watch it! I do not  want to get knocked out by your flailing."

I turned to apologize—and nearly stepped on a decomposing goose. The burst of stench that hit me was immediate and vile.

"Urghh—gross!" I gagged, leaping awkwardly over it. My foot landed ankle-deep in a patch of squelching, sour mud. "Oh COME on!"

Tatl winced as I yanked my boot free with a wet slurp. "That poor bird. How did it even get  here?"

"No idea," I muttered. "But I'm done with this. Let's move over there—before I end up face-first in a pile of swamp meat."

I stomped toward the tangled treeline, boots squishing, cloak dragging muck. Tatl zipped after me, trying to escape the thickest cloud of biting insects.

"And what if we run into monsters back there?"

"Honestly? I’d rather deal with a monster than trip over one more dead goose."

"That’s a bold strategy. 'Death by bog beast' versus 'death by bird blunder.' Truly heroic."

"We’re probably near the shop by now anyway. Monsters don’t usually hang around where there’s signs of people... right?"

Tatl glanced around the gloom. "This place hasn’t looked 'people-friendly' in a decade. But hey, if you say so, Hero."

"Relax. We’ve got this. Just stay close."

"Pfft...sometimes I wish I had your confidence," she muttered, landing delicately on my shoulder. Her wings drooped just a little. "But I guess after all the crap you’ve faced, a little murder-mud and monster-stench is nothing."

"Well, once you've been sucked inside a giant whale, travelled through time, defeated the literal King of Evil, or changed into the opposite gender, I think you'd be surprised at what you can accomplish. At that point there's really nothing that can surprise you."

Tatl let out a genuine laugh. "Okay, fair. You’ve earned your swamp swagger. Maybe I’ll borrow some."

"Just don’t borrow my boots. They’ve officially died."

"Duly noted. I'm glad I can fly."

She flexed with a tiny hum, mimicking a bodybuilder pose. I snorted.

"Alright, tough girl. Let’s tone it down—I think I see something up ahead. Building-shaped. Maybe we’re finally close."

I ran to the carved wooden structure ahead, heart pounding with each step, trying to stay silent as the trees loomed like sentinels around me. The encroaching darkness made everything seem warped and surreal. The gateway before me was a strange burst of color in the gloom—etched with painted owls, foxes, and glowing symbols in vibrant reds and blues. Beneath it, a worn sign read: "This way to Kotake's Magic Shop, for all your mystical needs!" The arrow beneath the cheerful script felt almost mocking.

"Well, I'm glad we're almost there. It's getting really hard to see out here, plus these flies are getting on my last nerves!" I slapped at my arm again, feeling the wet crunch of a crushed bug against my skin.

"I couldn't agree more, I was about to say that myself… I'm being attacked from all sides!" Tatl flailed her tiny arms like she was drowning in a storm of gnats.

We passed through the archway, and the air instantly grew heavier, wetter. The dirt path narrowed and twisted through choking underbrush, while moss hung like streamers from crooked limbs above. Somewhere to our right, an animal let out a guttural cry—abrupt, short, then silence. I paused. Tatl hovered close, glancing around with wide eyes.

"Probably just a wolfos," I whispered. "They hunt at dusk."

The light dropped sharply. What little sun remained was swallowed by the canopy above, casting long shadows that shifted as though breathing. Tatl nudged my arm, pointing at something ahead—a flickering orange glow weaving through the dark trees.

"Hey, what's that? You think it could be someone who needs help?"

"Umm, maybe... but we need to get to that shop."

"Yeah but... what if they're in trouble?" She fluttered anxiously toward the treeline. "C'mon, let’s go see!"

A flash of silver blurred past us.

With a deafening crack, a tree exploded in front of Tatl, showering her in bark and splinters. She screamed. My instincts kicked in—just as a massive, gnarled hand lunged from the shadows toward me. The skin was stretched taut over blackened bone, riddled with fungus and oozing pustules. I barely twisted away in time, rolling into the wet leaves and dirt, heart stuttering.

Odalwa. Again.

I bolted behind a tree as the ground shook beneath the thing’s steps. Tatl zipped upward, her glow panicked.

"Link! Watch out!"

The monster’s massive blade carved through the air, slashing through vines and trees as if they were tissue. It moved with terrifying purpose—this wasn’t a wild swing. It was hunting.

I dove behind a bush as the sword cleaved my previous hiding place clean in two. Wood detonated in splinters. Leaves shot upward in a burst of green and brown. The very air seemed to recoil, buzzing with static and the scent of burning rot. A guttural wail—inhuman and primal—rattled my bones, deep and resonant, like the cry of something ancient and wrong.

Then I saw its face.

The mask’s eyes turned toward me—twin voids of pitch black, bottomless and lightless, pulling in every flicker of the world around them. They weren’t just empty—they were devouring. Shadows bent toward them. My heart seized.

Black ichor oozed from the corners of the mask like tar-slick tears, dripping steadily onto the moss and hissing where it landed. Thin veins of fungus pulsed along its surface, glowing faintly like embers under skin.

It didn’t see me.

It sensed  me.

No time to transform.

I wrapped trembling fingers around my bow, notched a magic arrow, and aimed through the foliage. The faint glow lit up the brush—too late.

The creature's head snapped in my direction. It roared, louder than thunder. A wall of sound and fury.

And it charged.

I tried diving away back into cover as Odalwa charged toward me, flinging broken trees every which way like a tantrum of nature itself. One of the soaring trunks clipped me in the left shoulder, and the impact exploded in white-hot agony. I screamed, a raw and guttural sound, as my body twisted midair and slammed into the ground. I hit hard, my lungs seizing up, my vision clouding. Pain exploded through my shoulder in searing white-hot waves, each breath agony. My vision blurred with tears, limbs unresponsive, useless. Terror surged, bitter and choking. I had never felt this vulnerable—this powerless.. Every heartbeat sent another lance of torment down my side.

The ground shook as Odalwa’s massive footfalls closed the distance. My heart thundered. I blinked away tears, mud clinging to my face. And then—

It loomed.

The mask hovered inches from my face, steaming breath washing over me like a swamp fog from a rotting beast. The stench made me gag—sour, fungal, and metallic. Black ichor dribbled from its jaw, sizzling where it hit the earth. The hollow eye sockets stared into me—those unnatural voids that swallowed all light, all hope.

My limbs refused to move. My shattered shoulder sent waves of paralysis through me. My good hand scrabbled weakly at the dirt, reaching for a branch, a weapon, anything . But I couldn’t escape.

"Get away from me!" I shrieked, voice breaking with terror. I tried to slide back, but my body wouldn’t obey. My arm collapsed beneath me and I fell sideways into the muck.

The mask tilted.

The monster reached for me—its twisted hands gnarled like dead wood, dripping with ooze, its fingers twitching like it could already feel my throat.

And then—

Tatl.

She hurled herself in front of the mask, clutching a jagged splinter of wood. With a sharp cry, she drove it straight into the thing’s eye.

The sound was unbearable. A screech tore through the swamp like a siren, rattling the trees and my skull alike. Purple liquid jetted from the mask’s eye socket, spraying in thick arcs that hissed when it hit the dirt and plants. Odalwa stumbled back, claws clutching at the embedded shard, convulsing in rage.

Tatl dropped.

She fell limp, tumbling end over end until her tiny body hit the ground like a fallen ember. Her glow flickered erratically, fading.

"Tatl! Tatl, are you— gasp —okay!?"

I dragged myself toward her with my good arm, each movement a scream inside my nerves. Twigs tore my skin. Vines tangled my legs. But I didn’t care.

She was lying in a nest of debris, her little limbs trembling, her face smeared with that same grotesque purple ooze. Her breaths came in short, shallow bursts—barely there.

"Tatl! No, no no no—stay with me! Come on! Please!"

I reached out and cradled her tiny body in my palm, feeling her weak pulse through my dirty fingers. She didn’t respond.

I sobbed, helpless. The swamp pressed in from all sides.

“Tatl! Tatl, please—”

My voice broke, raw with panic and grief. I cradled her tiny, trembling form gently, fear squeezing my heart like a vise. I could lose anyone, anything—but not her. Never her.

Then—

The ground thundered.

Odalwa had recovered. It stood tall and furious, blade raised in a high arc. I couldn’t move. Couldn’t run. I had nothing left.

It let out a roar—a bellow of hate and hunger—and charged.

I closed my eyes, clutching Tatl to my chest.

And waited for the end.

An icy blast of freezing air suddenly whizzed past my face, so sharp and dry it sliced the breath from my lungs. A thunderous thwomp  followed, shaking the ground like a boulder had fallen from the sky. I dared to crack one eye open.

Odalwa’s massive, decaying form lay toppled, now encased in a thick, crystalline coffin of ice. Frozen mid-lunge, its blade still half-raised, the monster let out muted, muffled groans from behind the frost, its limbs twitching just slightly beneath the surface. Steam hissed from the seams of the block as the swamp’s heat began to eat away at the edges, but for the moment—blessedly—it was trapped.

Then she appeared.

The hag swooped in from the trees like a phantom wind, hunched low upon a crooked broomstick that left a curling trail of shimmering frost in the air behind her. Her robes were tattered and layered, adorned with glowing runes and stitched charms that jingled faintly as she moved. Frost clung to the hems of her sleeves. Her skin was pale green and wrinkled like dry leather, and a massive sapphire glittered from the center of her towering violet turban.

I blinked, dazed, something was familiar about her. Her presence buzzed through me like static, familiar yet distant—like a name on the tip of my tongue. Where had I seen her before?

With a guttural rasp, she raised her gnarled hands—decked in silver rings and bright lacquered nails—and chanted something low and ancient. The ground beneath the frozen monster rippled with crimson light, then split apart like paper. A swirling vortex of red magic yawned open, its edges crackling with raw power.

Odalwa groaned again, its massive body trembling within the melting ice. With a sharp gesture and a hissed word, the hag completed her spell.

The frozen brute sank into the portal without a sound. One moment it was there—the next, it was swallowed by the red void. The portal snapped shut with a hiss, leaving behind only curling steam and scorched moss. A surge of relief and awe washed over me as Odalwa vanished into the swirling portal, replaced by stunned silence. We had escaped death—barely, impossibly.

The witch exhaled through her nose, satisfied, and gave a brisk nod. The sapphire on her turban flashed. She turned toward us, muttering something unintelligible—something about reckless girls and unfinished errands —as she glided closer.

My body went limp.

With the danger gone, all the adrenaline drained from me at once. The world swam before my eyes. The pain in my shoulder roared louder than ever. I tried to hold onto consciousness, but it slipped through my fingers like mist.

The last thing I saw was her boots touching the ground beside me, and a gloved hand reaching down, glowing softly with pale blue light. As darkness claimed me, I clung to a fragile hope that this witch—this savior—had arrived in time. Tatl’s small body still nestled in my palm was the last thing I felt as everything slipped away.

Chapter Nineteen

Toil and Trouble

A dull ache radiated from my shoulder as I slowly regained consciousness, the throb of pain pulsing in time with my heartbeat. My body felt heavy, wrapped in fatigue, and the air around me was thick with a strange and spicy smell that clung to the warmth like smoke in an enclosed room. My limbs felt like they were submerged in warm honey, sluggish and uncooperative. I was lying on a soft mat, and heard the faint sound of bubbling liquid nearby—a rhythmic, soothing noise like a distant cauldron simmering with secrets.

Footsteps approached—soft, deliberate. Something warm and wet gently plopped onto my shoulder, followed by a tug as it was wrapped and secured. A spike of pain lanced through me, sharp and sudden, and I sucked in a gasp through clenched teeth.

My eyes flew open at the sting. The moss poultice pressed against my skin was warm and damp, its earthy, astringent scent blooming in the air like crushed herbs beneath a midsummer sun. I turned my head and tried to sit up, but a wrinkly, olive-toned hand pressed firmly against my chest, holding me down with surprising strength.

"Stay there, you foolish child!" a voice croaked. "Your body’s still healing. No need to go flopping about like a fish out of water."

I stared up at her—the same ancient hag who had frozen Odalwa in a block of ice. Her skin was cracked and weathered like bark, eyes bugged and sharp as beetles. She leaned over me, peering at my shoulder like it was a misbehaving experiment.

"My potions work fast, yes, but not that  fast. Hmph! You’ll be up and charging into danger again soon enough. You’re lucky I found you when I did, you know. Not every day I scoop a screaming girl and her twitching glowbug off my front lawn."

I opened my mouth to speak, but my throat was scorched and dry, like I’d been chewing Gerudo desert sand for hours.

"T-Tatl..." I rasped, barely a whisper.

The witch blinked. "Tattle? Tattle on who ?" she asked, squinting in suspicion.

I shook my head weakly. "My... my friend. Is she—cough, cough—is she okay?"

Understanding dawned in her expression. She gave a grunt and waddled over to a cluttered table, rummaging among jars and flasks. "Ah, the fairy. Tough little puff of light, that one. Took a direct hit of Odalwa’s blood. Nasty, toxic stuff, that. But you’re lucky, you see— I’m  the greatest alchemist in all Termina! If anyone could save her, it’s me."

A knot of fear in my chest loosened just slightly.

"She’s not out of the woods yet," the witch added, grabbing a clear flask filled with milky white liquid. "But she’s stable. Breathing. Needs lots of rest. So do you, by the looks of it."

I watched her closely as she turned, the light catching on the rings decorating her gnarled fingers. My gut twisted with doubt. After everything, could I trust her? Was this kindness... real? Or something worse hidden behind her craggy smile?

And yet, there was no real malice to her movements. Only practiced care. Purpose.

She returned to my side, holding the flask like a chalice.

"Here, drink this. It'll help to free  your parched tongue." The witch clucked softly, her tone lilting—but her grip on the flask was firm, insistent. She tilted the pungent substance to my lips without waiting for agreement.

The liquid was oddly sweet, thick with a flowery, perfumed essence that clung to my tongue. It tasted like violets crushed with honey and something faintly coppery beneath, like blood hidden beneath blossoms. As it slid down my throat, I felt the fire in my throat dim, the cracked dryness replaced with a soothing coolness. It relaxed me almost instantly. Too instantly.

I closed my lips around the edge and pulled more of the tonic into my mouth—until she jerked the flask away.

"Alright, alright, that's enough. I don't need you drinking too much and becoming all giddy on me." She set the flask back onto the nearby table, with a more stern gleam in her eye. "Now then, would you tell me what you're doing with that enchanted bow?"

There was a weight behind the question. Not anger. Not threat. But something colder—expectation. Like a teacher who already knew the answer, and was just waiting to see how honest I’d be.

The ancient woman gestured to a shadowed corner, and my eyes followed her crooked finger. The dim flicker of a candle revealed my clothing and blue bow, neatly draped over a wall-mounted hook like museum artifacts. Dust motes drifted lazily through the beam of light, catching on the fine hairs of hanging herbs and glittering jars that lined the stone walls. The room was steeped in an overwhelming earthy scent—moss, dried roots, sharp tangs of preserved bark and bitter oil. It felt old, dense with secrets.

Seeing my belongings—laid out, handled, distant—sent a shiver through me. I felt exposed, small. Like something picked clean and set aside for study. My eyes dropped reflexively to myself.

Bandages were tightly wrapped across the curves of my breasts, and a scratchy brown blanket clung to my hips and thighs. That was it. My legs, bare and cold beneath the thin wool, curled in instinctively. My cheeks flushed with heat.

The old crone caught my mortified glance and cackled like wind through brittle branches.

"Oh come now, hehehe, don't look so embarrassed. There isn't anything there I haven't seen before—not that you’ve got much to show, runt. Hardly any muscle at all. Now. About that bow."

Her sudden shift in tone—low and expectant—hit me like a jolt. I swallowed.

"It was given to me by the Great Fairy," I said quickly, the words slipping out faster than I meant. I couldn’t stop myself.

"Is that so... huh." Her eyes narrowed like a snake in tall grass. "I would've hoped that such a wise fairy queen had better judgment. Why would she give a stupid young girl like you  something so powerful?"

My stomach clenched. "Hey! I may have gotten ambushed by that creature out there, but I'm not stupid! The Great Fairy gave me that bow because she said I was the only hope for this world!" I pushed myself upright with trembling effort, only for her hand to slam me back into the bedding again. She was smiling. But there was nothing soft behind it.

"The only hope? Ha! If I hadn't been picking mushrooms out there you'd be lizalfos stew meat by now—or strung up with the others, wherever that beast takes them!" she barked, her grin sharp and wolfish. "So you better tell Ol' Kotake the real  story... before I turn you into a Deku toad."

My breath caught.

Upon hearing her say that name, a jolt of recognition stabbed through me—cold and immediate. The dark-skinned hag’s face blurred with a memory from deep within the Spirit Temple: fire, mirrors, laughter like shattered glass. Kotake and Koume—Ganondorf’s surrogate mothers. My breath caught as I recalled the way their magic had twisted minds, corrupted the Gerudo, and nearly destroyed me. No wonder her voice unsettled me the moment I heard it. She was one of Ganondorf's surrogate mothers! Kotake and Koume! Years ago, I had to fight the powerful witches in the Spirit Temple, back in Hyrule! Their magic had twisted the minds of the Gerudo, built the backbone of Ganondorf’s army, and nearly destroyed me
