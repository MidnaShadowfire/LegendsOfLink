Chapter Thirty-Six

The Stone Warrior

The bonfire’s light cast long, flickering shadows across the twilit grove, turning the moss-covered stones and gnarled roots into shapes that felt older than memory. Two Goron guards flanked me, their grips like iron clamps on my shoulders—unyielding, unshakable. I didn’t struggle. I knew better. Every inch of my body warned me that a single twitch could lead to shattered bones.

Around us, a low chorus of deep voices rumbled in the dusk: chanting, humming, something between mourning and ritual. I couldn’t see the source, but I felt the weight of it, vibrating softly through the earth beneath my boots. The ground thrummed with sorrow—slow, steady pulses like the heartbeat of something ancient and grieving. It was as if the very mountain remembered every loss ever suffered, and was mourning anew.

Each vibration carried something deeper than sound—grief, longing, a quiet despair that seeped up through the soles of my feet and wrapped itself around my chest. It wasn’t just the air that was heavy—it was the feeling of losing something precious.

I couldn't help but feel an oppressive sadness tighten around my own emotions, sinking into me like cold water.

But I didn't dare move to see what was happening, for fear of my bones being crushed with just a simple gesture from one of the massive hands keeping me still. The elder Goron, who I assumed was the leader of the tribe, slowly approached with a strange, yet somehow familiar, lens held in his gnarled, stony hands. The artifact shimmered faintly in the firelight, and it reminded me of something I had found in my past...but I couldn't place it.

Most of the Gorons I could see in the camp, younger and heartier, stood a meter or more above my height—yet the hunched elder's faded blue eyes were level with mine. Almost face to face now, he studied me with a weathered intensity that made my skin prickle.
Tatl hovered silently near my shoulder, unusually quiet. I could feel the tremble in her wings—not from fear, but from something deeper. She was feeling it too: the strange pull of sorrow that had thickened the very air here.

Then, with great care, he raised the lens between us.

It was definitely familiar... but also unlike anything I had ever seen. The frame was forged of dark volcanic iron, warped and weathered with age, wrapped in threads of silver that glinted like frost under the firelight. At its center pulsed a deep crimson gem, shaped like a watchful eye, its surface alive with shifting light. Runes spiraled along the metal edge—ancient script that shimmered faintly as if reacting to the presence of magic.

When he looked through it, the world felt like it was bending around me.

My own reflection shimmered in the gem—no, not just shimmered. It changed. Something beneath the surface stared back at me: not quite my face, not quite a stranger. The air grew heavier, and for a fleeting second I felt exposed—not just seen, but known.

Boromu's brow furrowed further, the lens trembling slightly in his grip. He lowered it slowly, as if reluctant, then cast a glance down my frame, pausing as if confirming something. Then he raised the glass eye again, the gem flashing with internal light.

"Curious... in all my years I've never seen such...."

His gravelly voice trailed off into muttering, thick with wonder and unease. He slipped the lens into his sash with a reverence usually reserved for sacred relics, his sagging lips twisting into a strange pout.

"Elder Boromu, what is it? What did you see?" one of the guards asked reverently, clearly surprised.

"This human... there is a strange conflict within them. Two souls shifting back and forth between this one, and a warrior male garbed in green. How could this be?"

The elder came closer, and I caught the heavy stench of sulfur on his breath. I tried not to gag as he came within an inch of me, inspecting me with a gnarled finger pointed at my face.

"I'm not... from around here. My name is Link. I came from a land called Hyrule. I was riding my horse through the forest when I was suddenly ambushed by a Skull Kid wearing a strange mask. I tried to fight, but ended up getting cursed into... well, what you see now."

When I mentioned Hyrule, the Gorons holding me notably stirred. Boromu merely gazed on with those old, weary eyes.... but he looked away at the mention of Skull Kid, giving me a moment to breathe freely. But when he turned back, something in his expression had hardened.

"Skull Kid... yes, I am familiar with his violent tricks. He came to our village days ago and demanded we serve him. I refused... and we've been suffering since. An eternal winter has fallen on our beloved mountains. Where there should be summer warmth, there are now blizzards and ice. We believe he tampered with our Divine Beast—a relic of a bygone era that controls weather."

He paused, his gaze narrowing as he studied me anew.

"And you say you came from Hyrule..."

There was a note of unease in his voice. He turned slightly away, as if grappling with memory.

"Our ancestors fled Hyrule long ago, seeking peace. That land was torn by chaos—wars, gods walking as men, men playing at gods. It was a place where stone cracked and rivers ran backwards. Many still speak its name only in whispers. That you survived such a place at all is... remarkable."

Boromu looked to the chanting circle, where the earth softly rumbled outward. The scent of scorched pine lingered faintly in the cold air, curling around the flickering shadows like smoke from a distant fire.

"Our greatest warrior, Darmani, went to confront the Skull Kid at Snowhead Temple, where we commune with the Beast. He sought to calm Goht and end this unnatural winter... but alas, he has met a terrible fate. I tell you this only because the lens does not lie. And according to the lens, you are trustworthy—however strange your cursed soul may be."

His eyes drifted back to me with a forlorn expression, his lips twisted into a mournful curl. The firelight etched deep lines into his stone face, and the weight of memory seemed to sag from his shoulders.

"Darmani, our last hope, is dying. Slowly turning to stone and crumbling under a curse. Our priests cannot undo it; the magic is too strong. We came to Clock Town seeking help, thinking the humans might have strong remedies... but they proved to be treacherous thieves. They stole my son, for reasons I still do not understand. Their ways are strange to us. They value gold over honor. Material over Spirit."

The wind shifted, carrying with it the scent of pine smoke and crushed herbs. The chanting in the distance deepened, as if the grove itself leaned in to listen.

Boromu's old, faded eyes locked on mine, and I felt like my entire soul was laid bare beneath them—past, future, and everything in between.

"And yet, you, a soul conflicted by curses as well, who came from the war torn land of the ancients... returned him to me. I must ask, and you will answer truthfully... why?"

I hesitated. For a heartbeat, the question echoed through me.

Because it was the right thing to do? Because I wanted to protect something? Because I couldn't save myself?

But all I said was:

"Because no one else would."

Boromu studied me for a long moment. Then he nodded once, slowly.

"Come. You will see Darmani. If the gods have placed you on our path, we shall follow it to the end."

The elder motioned with a wave of his hand, and the others released their vice-like grip on my shoulders. I rolled them back with a wince, only now realizing how tight their hands had been against my narrower build. The ache lingered like phantom bruises.

Boromu began his slow shuffle toward the chanting circle, and I followed close behind. The vibration grew stronger with each step, resonating through my boots and up into my spine. Yet the chants did not grow louder. It was as if the Gorons were no longer speaking to each other, but to the mountain itself.

Then, all at once, the chanting stopped.

The silence was absolute. Even the wind seemed to still. For a breathless moment, the grove felt like the eye of a storm—expectant, reverent, unnerving. The Ocarina in my satchel pressed gently against my hip, warm and faintly pulsing, as if aware of what was to come.

When we reached the edge of the circle, the assembled Gorons stepped aside in solemn unison. I crossed the boundary, and the earth gave a soft groan beneath my feet.

At the center of the circle lay a massive Goron, unmoving. His form stretched long and broad, his bulk weathered by snow and time. He was so still, he could have been a sculpture—but he was no work of art. This was a body resisting death with every grain of stone. Most of his skin had turned to a dark, mottled grey, fractured like cooling lava. Only a few patches of the original russet-gold peered through near the joints and chest. Tiny flakes of crumbling dust fell from his shoulders with each subtle tremor in the ground.

His face was locked in an expression of grim defiance—or was it pain? I couldn’t tell. Not until I stood before him did I realize how large he truly was. Even lying down, he towered over me.

The scent of burning pine and smoked sage still lingered in the air, curling low like ghosts around the stone circle. Clay pots of incense had been arranged with care near the giant's head, their embers glowing faintly as they sent delicate ribbons of smoke drifting through the cool breeze. The fragrances mixed with the earthy tang of moss and fire-kissed stone, creating a quiet sacredness that clung to the skin.

Boromu raised one arm slowly and gestured toward the fallen warrior.

"If you truly believe that you can help us, do so now."

My thoughts went to the Ocarina resting in my belt pouch, and I gingerly pulled it free, feeling the smooth, cool material against my fingertips. It pulsed faintly with memory. Tatl, uncharacteristically quiet, hovered near my shoulder, her wings a faint glimmer in the dusk. She said nothing, but her silence was weightier than words. When I brought the Ocarina to my lips, she floated back and gave me space—a rare, reverent stillness.

I knew what I had to do.

The notes of the Song of Healing flowed out in quiet ripples, soft and mournful. The melody hung in the grove like mist, echoing between the trees and the hearts of those gathered. Not a whisper could be heard. Even the fire seemed to bow to the music, its flames stilling just slightly with each note.

When the last note faded into silence, nothing happened at first.

The Gorons glanced around, unsure. The quiet felt heavier now, expectant. Then, a thin crack of light shimmered along the fault lines in Darmani's petrified skin—subtle, but unmistakable. Gasps rippled through the circle.

As if summoned by the song, the stone began to loosen. It crumbled and flaked away like old bark shedding from a tree, revealing the golden-brown hue of Goron flesh beneath.

The circle held its breath.

Darmani twitched. Barely. Then again. A finger curled. A massive chest lifted in a shallow breath.

And then, as if released by some ancient spell, the crowd exhaled in unison as the fallen warrior began to breathe again on his own.

Darmani opened his eyes, slowly, as the last of the stone crumbled away. Tatl drew close to me and with a relieved smile, released a held breath of her own.

"Link... you did it! The song worked this time!"

The Elder Goron's throat grumbled in relief, staggering up to Darmani's head. With a gnarled hand, Boromu brushed debris off the giant's face, smiling with a mouth that had hardly any teeth left.

"Darmani... what a gift this child has brought us. We are saved!"

But the moment shattered.

The giant opened his mouth as if to speak, but suddenly he gasped in pain, clutching at his side with a ragged, rattling breath. The sound was wrong—wet, labored, desperate. Boromu's smile froze and fell away as he looked where Darmani was grasping. His eyes, so recently bright with hope, turned to dread.

I followed his gaze.

Where once there had been only stone, now there was a deep, open gash. Blood, thick and dark blue, welled from it in sickening pulses, spilling across the mossy earth like ink from a cracked vessel. It soaked into the soil, steaming faintly in the cool air.

"No... no no no," Tatl murmured beside me, her wings dimming, her voice shaking. "That wasn’t supposed to happen. He was supposed to be okay!"

Boromu waved his cane at the circle, barking orders, calling for herbs, a dressing, anything to stop the flow. Gorons rushed forward with armfuls of green-smoked leaves and thick bandages, their heavy steps thudding desperately through the soil. But I knew. I could see it in Darmani's eyes—glassy, unfocused, already beginning to slip.

I knelt beside him, barely breathing, and raised the Ocarina again with trembling fingers.

"Please," I whispered, as much to the gods as to the instrument. "Not this. Heal him! Please...!"

I desperately brought the instrument to my lips again. The Song of Healing spilled out once more, trembling and raw. My breath hitched with every note, my hands unsteady, tears stinging behind my eyes. The song was slower this time—not a melody, but a plea. I played it again, and again... but nothing answered.

Around us, the circle of Gorons fell into a mournful silence, their expressions stunned and helpless. Some had fallen to their knees. Others covered their faces with thick hands. Even the fire seemed to dim.

But even before the final note echoed into silence, I felt it. The stillness.

Darmani exhaled one last time. A soft glow began to rise from his chest, pale and golden. The wind caught it, swirling the light upward as the massive form of the warrior began to dissolve, fragment by fragment, into shimmering dust. Darmani's eyes looked up at Boromu with a deep sadness—a sense of failure, of hopelessness—resonated from them. His lips parted, as if to speak, but no words came. Only a shuddering breath. And then... the light in his eyes faded, like coals cooling in a hearth.

"No..." I choked, my voice breaking.

I reached out without thinking, as if I could hold him here a moment longer — catch the light before it slipped away. My fingers brushed through warmth, through dust and radiance—until they met something solid. The sensation jarred me: not the hot, living skin of a warrior reborn, but the cool, resolute edge of carved stone. My hand, trembling, rested not on Darmani’s chest, but something else.

The light swallowed him.

Within seconds, only a faint outline of his body remained—and then, nothing.

Nothing but a single object remained, cradled beneath my trembling hand—resting gently where his heart had been.

A mask.

It bore Darmani's likeness in stone: solemn, strong, unyielding. The features were carved with grace and power, frozen mid-roar, like a silent monument to a warrior who had fought to the very end.

I curled my fingers around it, lifting it into my hands. The weight was immense—far heavier than its size should allow. The stone was still warm, as if holding the last breath of the one who had worn it.

One of the younger Gorons let out a choked sob and fell to his knees, pounding the earth in rhythmic grief. Another tore fistfuls of dirt from the ground and pressed them against his brow, weeping openly. An older Goron sank to his haunches and began to sing, low and broken—a mourning song that trembled through the grove like the dying echoes of a bell.

The silence was no longer reverent — it was hollow, raw, and vibrating with everything they had just lost. I took up the mask and held it close, its edges rough beneath my fingers, its weight deeper than stone. I didn’t know if I had just been given a weapon, a grave marker, or the burden of a dying warrior’s final wish. But I could feel it—etched into the stone, humming against my chest—that this was not the end. It was a beginning. And it wasn’t mine alone.

Chapter 37

The Mask of the Mountain

At first, there was nothing but silence. Even the roaring fire seemed to silence itself, feeling muffled in the heavy air. And then, a single snowflake fell.

Just one, drifting quietly from the darkened sky above the grove, landing without ceremony on the back of my hand. It clung there for a heartbeat—fragile, precise—before vanishing into my skin like a breath forgotten. Then another. And another.

Within moments, the first snows had begun.

It wasn’t fierce. Not yet. It was gentle, almost reverent, as though the mountain itself was bowing its head to grieve. The embers of the sacred fire crackled softly behind me, casting a fading warmth on my back. Around the circle, the Gorons remained kneeling or standing in stunned silence, their broad forms hunched beneath the weight of the moment.

Darmani was gone.

And in his place, I held a mask. As I stared at the mask, a tight knot of guilt twisted deeper into my chest. Darmani’s bravery left an echo in the air—reminding me uncomfortably of all the things I had failed to protect, all the lives slipping through my fingers like sand.

I hadn’t moved in what felt like hours. My hands still cradled the carved stone, rough and warm with lingering spirit. It didn’t feel like a mask. It felt like a heart carved hollow, still echoing the last breath of its owner.

A few snowflakes had settled on my head, melting immediately. The cold bearing down as if Darmani was the last bastion against the onslaught of winter. I let it. The cold seemed fitting.

Boromu stood nearby, his cane driven into the soft earth, his cracked eyes staring not at me, but at the hollow space where Darmani had vanished. He hadn’t said a word since.

The other Gorons were just as silent, but some of them held each other, others were broken down on the ground, faces in their hands. A young one pressed his forehead to the frozen earth, humming low and wordless. An elder had curled into himself, hands clutching smooth prayer stones etched in deep sigils, lips moving in a whisper too quiet to hear. Another Goron, broader than the rest, sat motionless with his hand outstretched toward the empty air where Darmani had disappeared, as if unwilling to believe the space was empty. Their grief filled the grove not with sound, but with presence—a pressure in the chest, a stillness in the trees, a heaviness in the snow as it began to gather.

Even Tatl seemed frozen—not from cold, but from reverence. Her wings fluttered just once as the snow began to fall, then stilled. The usual sharp glint in her voice had been smothered by the weight of what we’d witnessed.  Her glow dimmed to a pale shimmer, hovering uncertainly beside my shoulder. In that moment, even she seemed too fragile for words.

It felt like time itself had stopped. The snow fell in slow spirals, each flake tracing its own path toward the earth like forgotten prayers. The air tasted of smoke and frost. The fire crackled behind us, brittle and subdued, casting weak shadows across the grove. No one moved. No one breathed loud enough to disturb the hush.

Until Boromu finally moved.

The elder exhaled slowly, the sound more like a landslide than a sigh. Then he turned to me—not with authority, but with something heavier. Expectation. Hope, maybe. Or resignation. The furrows in his stone face were carved deep by grief, yet something flickered there—a dim ember refusing to die.

"If you carry his mask," he said, voice rough with age and grief, "then you carry what remains of him. Whether you asked to or not."

I didn’t answer. I wasn’t sure I could. My throat was tight, my thoughts brittle.

Snow began to gather in Darmani’s empty space—the last mark he’d left behind as he lay dying. It filled the depression slowly, gently, as if trying to erase the memory of his existence. But I couldn’t let it. I couldn’t look away. The swirl of snow blurred the edges of the hollow, like time trying to wash over pain too fresh to bury.

The grief that weighed on this grove wasn’t mine alone. It clung to my shoulders, settled into my lungs, mixed with the steam of my breath. I needed space to think, to understand why I felt so connected to these Gorons—why it hurt so much to watch them break.

Tears welled behind my eyes before I realized they’d been building. I turned from the fire’s last embers, retreating to the edge of the grove. There, beneath a leaning pine glazed in frost, I stopped. My hands clutched the stone Goron mask, cold and sharp like it was Darmani's resolve. Its expression—frozen in strength, carved in roar—felt like a question I couldn’t answer.

The chill seeped in through my boots and into my chest, a creeping cold I couldn’t shake. Snow settled across my shoulders like quiet hands. I shivered hard, and then I felt Tatl’s glow at my shoulder—a faint pulse of warmth against the wind, like the last ember in a fading hearth.

“Link… are you okay?”

Her voice was soft. Not teasing. Not biting. Just… worried.

I shut my eyes and looked away, jaw tight.

“I… I don’t know,” I whispered, my strength breaking. “This… this wasn’t supposed to happen. I keep losing, Tatl. I keep trying and nothing works. Ever since I left Hyrule... it’s like the gods are angry at me. Like they’re taking things away from me. One by one.”

My voice faltered, choked by the sharp sting rising in my throat. “And maybe... maybe they are. Maybe I turned my back on what I was meant to be. Maybe I abandoned the very path they gave me, and now they’ve abandoned me in return.”

I could feel it, like a door had quietly closed somewhere far behind me. A divine silence. Not punishment. Not wrath. Just absence. The terrifying weight of being left alone—not by people, but by purpose.

The mask remained in my hands, unmoved. But something in its silence felt familiar. Like it knew that same emptiness.

And all I could hear was the wind whispering through the pines and the snow collecting the heavy silence like stones—patient, inevitable. It was as though the world itself had run out of answers. I stared into the mask, and the mask stared right back, almost as if in defiance to my weakness.

And then, it pulsed.

A slow, low vibration thrummed through my palms—not painful, but resonant, like a struck bell echoing through stone. The mask shimmered faintly, its eyes briefly catching the starlight. I gasped and almost dropped it.

"Did you feel that?" I whispered.

Tatl hovered a little closer, her glow dimming in awe. "Feel what? Are you—"

But before she could finish, the world shifted.

A rush of heat filled my chest, then my skull. The wind around us seemed to stop entirely. I blinked—and the pine forest vanished.

I was standing on a mountaintop.

Snow swirled in blistering waves around me. A horn sounded in the distance, deep and mournful. Goron voices echoed in a ritual chant, and then shattered into screams. I turned—behind me, a temple of stone and ice cracked open as a monstrous shape emerged, bound in chains of lightning and frost.

Goht.

I felt the bitter, biting cold of the mountaintop as if it were my own, tasted the coppery tang of blood from a wound I hadn't earned, and knew the grief and fury of a warrior staring down his final battle, knowing he would not survive, yet fighting all the same

I could feel the memory like it was mine . The cold. The fear. The duty. I was seeing through Darmani’s eyes—his final memory, left behind in the mask like a song trapped in stone. His last desperate stand. His failure. His sorrow.

And beneath it all... a plea.

The vision fell away as quickly as it had come. I staggered forward, heart hammering in my chest. The mask’s warmth faded, but the echo remained.

Tatl hovered close again, breathless. “Was that…?”

I nodded.

“He wants me to finish it. He wants someone to carry it through.”

The mask rested in my hands, still and heavy once more. But it no longer felt like just a relic. It felt like a bond. A burden passed through stone and time.

And I knew, whether I was ready or not—this was the path I would walk.

My fingers tightened around the stone edges. Without thinking, as if guided by something deeper than will, I lifted the mask.

"Link, wait—"

Tatl’s voice didn’t reach me in time. The moment the mask touched my face, the world twisted.

A surge of heat slammed into my chest. My arms jerked backward, spine arching unnaturally as a tremendous force tore through my muscles, pulling my body outward, upward, wider . My skin split and reshaped itself in impossible ways, bones snapping and thickening, pain laced with something almost euphoric. My tunic burst at the seams. My boots cracked open. The mask fused to my face like molten iron—and then, like stone, it became me. My mind splintered under the surge of raw strength, both terrifying and exhilarating. Link—my true self—was slipping away, replaced by a powerful certainty that burned in every muscle and bone.

When I hit the ground, it cracked beneath me.

I gasped, but the sound that escaped my throat was no longer mine. It was a bass-heavy grunt , deep and gurgling like a landslide. I looked down—my arms were huge. Thick slabs of muscle covered in rough stone skin. My chest heaved, wide as a wagon.

“Hurr… what… happenin’…?” I mumbled. Even my voice was different—deep, guttural, and dim. My mouth wasn’t moving the way I expected. My tongue felt too big.

"Oh… holy gods," Tatl murmured, darting around my shoulder. "You're... You're a Goron! But... wow. A huge  one."

I blinked slowly, my vision strange and heavy—like everything had slowed, just a little. My thoughts felt... distant. Sluggish. Like someone had packed cotton around my brain.

I looked down at myself—at the broad, domed chest rising and falling like a boulder shifting with breath. My arms were massive, corded with muscle that looked less like flesh and more like carved basalt. My fingers ended in thick, ridged knuckles, like hammers shaped from stone. My once-loose tunic was now tattered cloth clinging awkwardly to the edges of a rocky torso, and my boots—what was left of them—had split wide to reveal massive feet ringed with stone-like ridges.

A glowing sigil, faint but pulsing with each heartbeat, burned softly on my shoulder. My entire body radiated heat, like smoldering stone after a volcanic surge. I could feel strength in every movement, but control was slipping away, thought by thought. I was hulking. Heavy. A wall of living rock.

Somehow, it all felt familiar.

My thoughts were thick. Like packed snow. But there was fire in my veins now. Pride. Duty. Something primal. The mask had given me this form for a reason—and I would use it. Smash ice. Protect tribe. That’s what Darmani wanted. That’s what I wanted . Right?

I notice the brothers standing behind me, looking at their hero reborn.

Elder Boromu speaks, his voice quivering with pride.

“Darmani? Is that… -no, no you’re different. You wear green garb and strange armor. Are you… are you truly that human who played the melody that heals? We all felt its power… heard its wisdom. Darmani passed, but not in a way any of us thought possible in a long, long time. He gave his strength for you to borrow as gratitude for healing his spirit, through that mask. Through you… he can save us!”

I felt honor and pride well up deep within my stones, my deepest hearts, and I raised a gigantic fist and roared back against the winter creeping up on us. I would be victorious! I would win! I would fight all threats that came my way! Suddenly I almost remembered what I was doing just moments before.

I turned to Tatl, confused. She hovered in silence for a moment, then groaned.

“Oh no. Not just a Goron… You’re him . You’re Darmani. Or, uh… something very close. Just… maybe not all the way there. ”

"Not, Darmani…no. Link, uh..I am… Huh...?"

I scratched my enormous head. My arms felt like cliffs. My hands—barely hands—were slabs of stone wrapped in muscle. Something was fuzzy. Not right. Thoughts made no sense. But I remembered that I had to smash winter! Break the ice!

"Me… s’posed to smash ice now, right?"

Tatl buried her tiny face in her hands. “This is going to be exhausting. ”

Already, I could feel something shifting inside me—sliding deeper. Thoughts grew slower. Simpler. Each word like a boulder I had to roll uphill.

I blinked. Tried to count. Wait… why count? Numbers were hard.

The world felt louder. Heavier. My heartbeat thudded in my ears like war drums.

Time passed oddly. A few minutes, maybe more. I tried to remember what I was doing… then forgot what I was remembering.

"RAHHH! WHERE ICE? ME BREAK BAD ICE!" I bellowed suddenly, slamming my fists together with a sound like thunder rolling through stone.

Tatl flinched, wings buzzing with alarm. Her glow flickered, unstable, and she backed away slightly. "Okay, that’s new. Link? Can you still hear me in there? Please, say something normal. Say anything."

"Link? Me Link! ME STRONG ROCK GUY LINK , HA!"

I laughed, big and echoing—like boulders laughing. Joke was strong. Me strong. Joke good.

But Tatl didn’t laugh.

She zipped in front of my face, her wings trembling now. "No no no—Link, come on. Look at me. Please look at me. You’re scaring me. This isn’t you."

Her tiny hands reached forward, as if trying to grab my attention, to shake me out of it. Her voice cracked. "You’re losing it. You have to take the mask off before—"

Her light darted out of reach, her voice rising in panic now. "No—don’t do that! Link, please! You’re slipping too far—don’t leave me here alone!"

The strength shake. My knees fall. Cracks through vision like stonebreak.

I fall. Grow weak. Lose seeing… fall…

A deep groan escaped me as my body lurched forward, the mask peeling off my face. The world tilted. I collapsed with a thunderous thud, cracking the frozen dirt beneath me. A plume of powdery snow burst outward from the impact, and for a heartbeat I felt like the mountain itself had dropped through my chest.

The strength drained from me like sand through open fingers. My breath came in short, ragged gasps. The mask… it pulsed once beneath me, a strange, low throb like a heartbeat caught in stone. It was warm—not comforting, but alive, like a soul remembering it had once breathed. The sensation ran up my spine like an echo, not painful, but not gentle either—more like a distant voice calling through bone.

A final surge of heat spread through my chest like a dying heartbeat. My body began to shrink. Stone skin softened, splitting like a shell. Limbs curled inward, bone and sinew twisting and pulling tight as if retreating from the world. My spine bent sharply, ribs aching as my frame folded into itself. The weight of that other form peeled away in layers, and my mind reassembled itself in jagged flashes—thoughts stuttering into words, language reforming like ice cracking into sharp patterns.

Then came the sudden cold.

The sudden absence of that hulking strength was dizzying. Where once my center of gravity felt like a boulder, now I was a tangle of delicate limbs and aching joints. My breath fogged in the air. My shoulders folded as snow began to gather on me. My body shivered so hard I could hear my teeth knock together. I felt exposed. Small. Like a leaf that had just fallen from the tree I had been part of seconds ago.

I lay there, panting, my fragile body curling in on itself. The mask rested beside me like a spent heart, still faintly warm against the frost.

"...Tatl?" I croaked.

She hovered above me, blinking rapidly. Her glow was dim, uncertain. “Oh thank the gods... You’re back. Just barely.”

Her wings trembled midair, and she darted in close, circling me once with nervous urgency before settling just beside my face.

“I didn’t know if you’d come back,” she whispered, her voice cracking. “You were gone, Link. Like… really gone. I was calling you and you didn’t even blink…”

I blinked slowly, breath still shaking. My limbs felt like water, my muscles empty of anything but exhaustion.

“Up ahead,” he continued, “there is a chamber of meditation, used by our young as the first rite to connect with sacred Snowhead. We can seal ourselves within. It will offer sanctuary to rest in peace.”

He lifted his cane and gestured down the corridor, his expression solemn.

“We will stop there for a time. Regain our strength. As we are now, we are no match for the shadows that stalk these halls.”

I nodded, grateful for the pause. My arms ached from the weight of my form, and the hunger gnawed at me like an echo in my gut. . I vaguely wondered if I was able to eat rock as in Goron form—or if I’d even want to try—but the thought passed quickly.

We pressed onward.

The silence in the halls deepened with every step, broken only by the soft rattle of wheels and the distant hiss of cooling lava veins. The mountain no longer roared. It whispered. And the whisper was wrong.

The passages widened into vast, echoing caverns veined with molten arteries. But what should have been a roaring forge of life felt... wrong. I could feel the heat—yes—but it was uneven, unfocused. Not a broiling heart of a mountain… but something strangled. Something decaying.

Rivers of once-living lava crawled in sluggish streams along the channels, their surface crusted in flaky obsidian scabs. The color was all wrong—no fierce gold, no blazing red. Only a muted, muddy orange glimmered through the fractures. The veins pulsed faintly beneath the cracked stone like a heart trying to remember how to beat.

It felt like walking through the ribcage of a god that had recently stopped breathing.

Boromu furrowed his brow, placing an ancient hand on one of the walls. His stone-scarred fingers brushed the blackened surface, reverent and mournful.

"This place once roared with heat," he muttered grimly. "Now it fades like a dying heart. The corruption reaches far here… even the breath of Vah Kel’Darah grows shallow."

Behind us, the Draumorim walked slower now. Their proud frames dimmed by something more than weariness—grief. Their gaze wandered not forward, but upward, to the stalactites and carved reliefs that once marked this as sacred ground. The loss wasn’t just physical—it was spiritual.

Even Tatl said nothing. She drifted onto my shoulder like ash on cooling coals, her glow dimmed. She didn’t fidget. She didn’t tease. She only rested there, quiet and watchful.

"Not far now. We will rest at the next chamber. Let’s move, my brothers. Time for mourning will come later," the elder sighed, a long landslide of a breath.

I swallowed the tightness in my throat and pressed on, taking the lead again. Sweat beaded on my temples, but I didn’t complain. I welcomed the heat, no matter how strange it felt in here. It was better than the strength-sapping blizzard outside. It felt… earned. If nothing else, it reminded me I was still alive. Still moving.

The shadows danced against the walls with every ripple of failing magma. Somewhere deep in the rock, I thought I heard it—like a groan. A low, vibrating sorrow. The kind of sound stone makes when it’s trying not to break.

As we passed under a steep archway with a familiar symbol of an eye and teardrop carved above it elegant, flowing lines. That symbol had always meant hidden truth, sacred witness. But here, it felt like a cry frozen in stone.

We ran into a solid obsidian door, flawless and lightless, absorbing the flickering glow around us. Elder Boromu took out the sacred Eye of Truth from his waist satchel, his hands steady despite the tremble in the air. He held it up to his faded vision, the lens catching the light in a single flash. After a moment of searching, he placed his ancient hand on the smooth obsidian.

A pulse. A soft ripple of blue light shimmered outward from his palm like water disturbed in a bowl. With a low, resonant grinding of stone against stone, the door began to rise, vanishing into shadow above us.

The air inside shifted.

It was cooler in here. Not the life-giving warmth of fire, but the heavy, dormant pressure of something long buried. The walls arched into a high dome, and the ceiling had been carved and painted with faded murals—spirals of snow and ice, rising flame and heat, figures of Goron pilgrims bowing before the mountain. Life flowing abundantly in a continuous arc from the heart of the mountains. On top of a the tallest peak stood a lone figure of an animal... yet not quite animal. It almost looked like a machine or statue of a four legged beast of burden. A quiet reverence lingered here, dulled by time but not yet erased.

In each corner of the chamber stood what looked to be Sheikah Stones, but larger—shaped like seated Gorons. Their broad shoulders and thick arms were stylized into the stone itself, each one wearing a mask adorned with the Eye and Teardrop. But the shape was different—sharper, angular, echoing Goron craftsmanship. The ancient alliance made manifest in stone.

These statues almost felt... awake.

There was a sense—just beneath hearing. A pulse in the rock. The silence here was not mere quiet—it felt sacred, mournful, like a held breath in a room of silent prayers. The stone walls, etched with memories of devotion, seemed to whisper softly of hope still flickering, though buried deep beneath sorrow.

“We will rest here, Rhomar Link,” Boromu said quietly, as if speaking too loudly would disturb the slumbering spirits carved into the stone. He turned, eyes shadowed. “Gromi, take Korozi and Belgor, and ensure the doors ahead are sealed.”

“Yes, Elder.”

Gromi—the towering brother who had once dragged me into their camp—grunted low and signaled two others. Without a word, they moved into the corridor beyond, the glow of their crystals bobbing like will-o'-the-wisps before vanishing into the dark. The sound of stone groaning shut came soon after, echoing like a distant memory.

I let out a breath I hadn’t known I was holding. The stillness was thick but not dead. It was watching.

“It is done, Elder. Korozi, set up camp, everyone gets rations.” Gromi’s voice rumbled low like rolling boulders. He turned toward me, his face weathered but earnest. “Rhomar, it may not be very tasty for humans, but we do have a type of bread that you both may be able to stomach if you are hungry. It’s important that you all keep up your strength. The path only gets more brutal ahead. Especially for a human.”

My stomach gave a sharp pang at the mention of food… I must have been far hungrier than I realized now that we had a moment of peace and safety.

“Thank you,” I said, bowing my head slightly. “I’m sure your food will be just fine. I appreciate the generosity.”

Gromi nodded once with a quiet grunt and turned to help the others. The Draumorim moved with quiet efficiency—despite their size, there was grace in how they handled the space. Belgor unrolled thick hide mats from a weathered pack, each marked with geometric red-and-black patterns. Korozi, stocky and broad-chested with an intricately braided beard ringed in bits of obsidian, tended to a small campfire built within a carved stone basin, stoking it with chunks of slow-burning coal that hissed faintly as they caught. The air smelled of mineral smoke and warm earth.

Tumar hovered near the cart, his young face tight with anxiety. He kept stealing glances at the mural above, then back to the older Gorons, his posture stiff with the pressure of expectation. Though his limbs still carried the weight of youth, he held himself as tall as he could, as though his spine alone could carry the honor of the tribe. Maro remained as still as a boulder in the cart.

Tatl poked her head out from my cloak, her glow dim but steady. "That bread better not be made of rocks. I swear if it cracks my teeth, I’m going to scream."

Ratt let out a soft laugh, wiping any frost that lingered from his cheeks and hair. "After that hike? I’d chew a tree stump if it came with salt."

"Haha Tatl, you don't even eat! Why would you care?" I laughed and gave her a playful tap with a finger.

"Hey I eat! You just never notice. I don't need as much as you huge lumbering humans." Tatl rolled her eyes and pushed my finger away gently.

I smiled, the weariness briefly softened by their banter. A portlier Goron named Dolmi—his arms tattooed with silver spiral bands and his eyes almost comically large—handed out dense, clay-colored loaves from a leather pack. The scent was earthy and faintly sweet, like roasted chestnuts and ash.

Ratt took his with a skeptical glance. "Looks like a rock. Smells like a rock. Here goes nothing."

He bit in—and blinked.

"Okay that’s… not bad actually. Kinda nutty. And spicy?"

"Mountain pepper-root," Dolmi rumbled helpfully. "Grows in the ashfall. Warms the chest."

Tatl took a hesitant nibble from mine, then widened her eyes in surprise. "Well, paint me surprised. Not bad for boulder bread."

I settled down beside Ratt on one of the thick hides. Heat curled around us in gentle waves, cocooning us momentarily in a peaceful, almost sacred hush.

The Gorons moved slower now, exhaustion beginning to catch them. Gromi sat against the far wall, arms crossed, his heavy lids fluttering as if in meditation. Korozi rubbed at his knees and murmured something in a rhythmic chant, the words low and ancient, like stones grinding underwater. Even Belgor, stoic and thick as a gatehouse, took a seat and traced an old carving on the stone floor with a calloused finger, lost in thought.

In the corners of the room, the ancient Sheikah-Goron effigies still watched, their silent masks reflecting the firelight and crystal rods with a dull gleam. Faint vibrations still hummed from within them—steady, patient, as though waiting for someone to speak the right word.

I watched them. This wasn’t just a rest—it was mourning. This place, once sacred, had been defiled. Their silence wasn’t just tiredness. It was grief. A quiet vigil for something they all felt.

I decided to take a chunk of the hard loaf. The crust cracked apart like a soft clay brick, flaking gently in my gloved hands. The inside was dense—spongy like moss and a deep volcanic gray. I took an experimental nibble. Surprisingly, it was tasty, with a hint of oaky spice and a slow-burning heat that bloomed across my tongue. I took a bigger mouthful, and my stomach growled in gratitude. The bread was heavy and tough, almost like dried meat, and every chew fed something deeper than just my body. It reminded me of hearth smoke and old forest bark—simple, honest things.

Beside me, Ratt had gone quiet. He chewed slower, distracted, then began to hum.

The tune floated gently out of him, soft and sorrowful—a lullaby woven from glass and elegance. Each note drifted like currents in still water, brushing against the edges of memory like wind through an old curtain. It was beautiful in its simplicity, ancient in its grace. My breath hitched, caught on the edge of recognition.

I knew that melody.

Not the name. Not the shape of it. But something inside me stirred—like a memory half-frozen, beginning to thaw.

I turned to him, my brow furrowed. "Where did you learn that song? It sounds… familiar. What is it?"

He blinked, startled by the question. "Oh. Uh… I dunno, actually. It’s just… been in my head since yesterday. Just lingering in the back of my mind... You know?"

I nodded slowly, the ache in my chest returning, I felt like I was forgetting something vitally important.

"Interesting… I swear I’ve heard it before… somewhere…"

Ratt looked down at the bread in his lap. His fingers were still now. He spoke more quietly, like he was speaking to himself.

"It makes me feel… sad. Like this place. Like something old was lost here. I don’t know why, I just… felt like it was right to hum. Like it belonged here. Something royal and noble to sing into this place."

I swallowed the lump in my throat with another bite of bread. "Sing it again," I said, voice hoarse. "Please. I don’t know why, but… it’s calming. I need to hear it."

One of the brothers passed around a large heavy stone flask, and I took it with both hands and swallowed a mouthful of pure, fresh spring water. I passed it to Ratt, and he smiled with a flicker of something solemn behind his usual smirk. After a few swallows of the revitalising water, he began to hum again—this time louder, surer. Each note lingered like a quiet echo, curling gently around memories buried too deep to face. I felt the ache of her absence, the gentle sadness of a goodbye left unfinished. The lullaby wrapped around me, soft as snowfall, warm as tears. The Draumorim turned their heads, listening with eyes closed, reverent.

The sound moved through the chamber like water rippling upward. It didn’t just echo—it filled the space. Sank into the bones of the stone. A melody of peace, but not without pain. It felt like starlight in winter.

A name surfaced in my mind. Just barely.

When Ratt finished, he let out a breath and whispered—barely audible, but I caught it.

"Zelda."

My hand froze. My entire body went still.

"Wait," I said sharply, turning to face him fully. "How do you know that name?"

Ratt’s eyes widened. "What name? Zelda?" He hesitated, clearly uncertain. "I… don’t know. I just think of that name when I hear that song. It fits. Like a memory trying to come through a fog. Why, who is Zelda? Do you know them?"

My mouth went dry. I looked into the flickering firelight and saw her—not her face, not exactly. Just the shape of what we’d shared. The ache of walking away from it.

"She…" I paused, searching for words that didn’t exist to describe how I was feeling. "She was a princess I once saved. We were close. Maybe more than close. But I chose to leave. I couldn’t—" I faltered. The truth was heavier than I expected. "Ugh… nevermind. It’s in the past."

Ratt looked at me carefully. Not with pity. Just understanding.

"You… couldn’t be satisfied in a time of peace," he said gently. "There was too much pain."

The words hit like a hammer to the chest. My eyes widened—not in anger, but in raw, unguarded shock.

How could he see that?

He shouldn’t have been able to. Not unless… he felt the same way somehow?

The firelight flickered between us, casting long shadows that danced across the ancient stone walls. Around the chamber, the low murmurs of the Gorons had faded into the hush of rest, but between me and Ratt, something raw and electric still lingered.

I looked down at my hands—slender, with fine wrists and graceful fingers, delicate in a way that I still wasn't used to. The fingerless brown gloves clung tightly to my skin, worn and weathered from the road. A Hero... That title meant less and less with every step into this frozen, cursed land. But somehow—here, now—his words made it feel less like a wound and more like a scar. Something that had bled… but was now starting to heal.

For a breath of a pause, I couldn’t speak. The fire crackled softly beside us.

And for the first time in a very long while, I felt like someone finally understood.

"Y… yeah," I finally whispered.

It was all I could say.

And somehow, in the echo of that quiet, it was enough.

The heaviness of the chamber and memory weighed on me, and I took a final bite of my portion of Goron bread, chewing it in silence. The flavor lingered—smoky, nutty, warm. It clung to my mouth like the heat of the fire still radiating from the center of the room. I held it there, letting the comfort settle as if to anchor me in the now.

“Alright, well if you two are done making out, I’m gonna get some sleep,” Tatl grumbled in irritation, fluttering off my shoulder in a blur of white gold. She curled into a glowing ball on the nearest bedroll, her wings twitching faintly like the last flickers of a firefly.

I frowned at her, exhaling with a slow sigh. The weight in my chest hadn’t lifted—if anything, it had simply rearranged itself. I swallowed the last of my bread and rose to my feet, moving toward the bedroll beside her. The stone beneath me felt colder now, even with the chamber’s residual warmth pressing into my skin.

I wasn’t tired. Not even close. The memory had shaken me loose. My mind was racing with too much I couldn’t untangle—anger, longing, sorrow, fear. All of it mixing in my blood like coals refusing to die out. This meditation chamber, carved for peace and self-reflection, only amplified what I was already carrying. It didn’t soothe me. It made everything louder.

“Yeah, that’s a good idea. I haven’t slept in days, it feels like,” Ratt said quietly after a long pause. His voice was softer than before, worn down by the day. He stood, brushing crumbs from his gloves, and walked slowly to a bedroll farther off.

I watched him go. Just for a second. I didn’t want him to leave. I wanted him beside me—to feel his warmth again, to borrow just a little of the steadiness he always seemed to summon. But the pang of guilt twisted deeper. Anger followed it, and I shut my eyes hard against both.

No. Not now.

We were still in danger. This place was still corrupted. And sleep—even restless, haunted sleep—was something I had to force if I wanted to stay strong enough to keep him... us... alive.

So I lay back on the hide mat, my bare fingers curling against the stout leather, and willed myself to stillness.

The chamber sighed around us, deep and old.

Somewhere beyond these walls, the mountain slowly died in silence.
